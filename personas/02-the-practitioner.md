# The Practitioner

*"I use AI every day now. I'm still figuring out when to trust it."*

## Who You Are

You crossed the starting line. You use Claude, ChatGPT, or Copilot regularly -- for drafting, analysis, research, brainstorming. Some outputs surprise you. Others disappoint. You're developing intuition for when AI helps and when it hurts, but the calibration is still rough. You know "prompt engineering" is a thing, but you also sense it's already being replaced by something deeper.

You're in BCG's "Explorer" archetype and McKinsey's "Bloomer" segment (39% of the workforce). You're past the 3-week abandonment cliff. The habit is forming. Now you need to sharpen it.

## Type of Work

Knowledge work with growing AI augmentation: emails drafted by AI and refined by you, research synthesized faster, meeting summaries automated, documents reviewed with AI as a second pair of eyes. You still do the final judgment work manually, but the production work is increasingly delegated.

## Pains

- **The 70% problem.** AI gets you to a 70% draft fast. [Getting from 70% to 95%](/videos/iG_CCjdyeX0) is where the real skill lives -- and where most people either accept mediocre output or give up entirely.
- **Calibration drift.** Every model release moves the capability frontier. [What you learned 6 months ago is already wrong](/videos/RnjgLlQTMf0). You need a continuously updated sense of what AI handles well and where it fails in *your* domain.
- **Context assembly.** Dumping an entire document is amateur hour. Providing nothing is worse. The skill is curating context with the care you'd use [briefing a talented new hire](/essays/2026-03-13-02-the-bicycle-problem).
- **Rejection weakness.** You accept output that "looks right" more often than you should. [The other 17%](/videos/-FhtPUkXKO4) -- the gap between "looks right" and "is right" -- is where AI output goes from useful to dangerous.

## Mindset

Growth-oriented and pragmatic. You've internalized that [clarity of thought is the real skill](/essays/2026-03-13-01-start-with-ai-today), not prompt tricks. You're willing to iterate. Your challenge is developing the judgment layer: knowing *when* output is wrong and *why*, then encoding that knowledge into reusable constraints.

## Psychological State

- **Productive discomfort.** You're past the identity threat but still in the adjustment period. Some tasks feel faster; others feel harder. The [METR study](/videos/bDcgHzCBgmQ) found experienced developers are 19% slower with AI on certain tasks while believing they're 24% faster -- overconfidence is a real risk at this stage.
- **Building confidence.** Each successful AI-assisted deliverable reinforces the habit. Each failure teaches calibration. You're accumulating what matters: frontier recognition.
- **Emerging taste.** You're starting to distinguish between AI output that's genuinely good and output that merely sounds confident. This "rejection competency" is [the most undervalued skill](/essays/2026-03-13-02-the-bicycle-problem) of the era.

## Attitude Toward AI

Enthusiastic but calibrating. You've moved past the novelty phase into practical integration. You don't evangelize to colleagues -- you just quietly get more done. Your constraint library is growing, one rejection at a time.

## The Path Forward

The Practitioner's next level depends on which direction their work takes:
- If you're building software → focus on specification quality and production readiness
- If you're applying AI to a specific domain → deepen context assembly and start encoding domain rules
- If you're managing teams → begin thinking about delegation frameworks and agent workflows
