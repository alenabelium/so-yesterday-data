# The Observer

*"I know AI is important. I just haven't found the right moment to start."*

## Who You Are

You are a knowledge worker -- manager, administrator, analyst, consultant, or professional -- who follows AI developments from a distance. You read headlines, maybe attend a company demo, and nod along when colleagues talk about ChatGPT or Claude. But when you sit down at your desk, you work the way you always have. Not because you're against AI. Because nobody showed you how to use it in a way that actually matters for *your* work, and the one time you tried, the output felt generic and underwhelming.

You might be the most conscientious person on your team. That's part of the problem. You ask "Are we allowed to use this for client work?" and, hearing silence, you opt out. Ironically, the less careful colleagues keep going.

## Type of Work

Traditional knowledge work: reports, analysis, presentations, project management, client communication, document review. You're productive in the current system. The work gets done. But the volume is increasing, the expectations are rising, and the hours feel less sustainable each quarter.

## Pains

- **Information overload without actionable clarity.** You've read about AI transforming work but nobody explains what that means for *your* specific role on *your* specific team.
- **The 3-week cliff.** A [Microsoft study of 300,000 AI users](/videos/EZ4EjJ0iDDQ) found that 80% abandoned the tools within three weeks. Not because the tools failed -- because nobody taught the judgment layer: when to trust output, when to push back, how to decompose tasks.
- **Growing anxiety.** You see colleagues becoming visibly faster. You hear about [entry-level hiring dropping 67%](/videos/bDcgHzCBgmQ). You sense the ground shifting but can't articulate what to do about it.
- **Effort-value confusion.** Your weekly report takes eight hours, and those eight hours feel productive. Having an AI draft it in ten minutes [feels like cheating](/essays/2026-03-13-02-the-bicycle-problem) -- even when you could spend the remaining time on higher-value judgment work.

## Mindset

Cautious, conscientious, risk-averse. You equate going slow with being responsible. In McKinsey's framework, you're a "Gloomer" -- not hostile to AI, but unconvinced it applies to you yet. The bicycle analogy fits: you think going slower is safer, when in fact [speed is what creates stability](/essays/2026-03-13-02-the-bicycle-problem).

## Psychological State

- **Permission gap.** Waiting for institutional or personal permission that isn't coming. "Many people are waiting for someone to tell them it's okay to work differently. No one is coming."
- **Comfort of familiar struggle.** There's a strange psychological safety in doing things the hard way. Your eight-hour report feels like proof of value.
- **Compounding anxiety.** The gap between you and colleagues who started [widens every quarter](/videos/pOb0pjXpn6Q). You sense this but convert the anxiety into more waiting rather than action.

## Attitude Toward AI

Not hostile. Curious but paralyzed. You believe AI will matter. You just don't believe *today* is the day to start. Tomorrow always feels safer. Meanwhile, the [compounding gap grows](/videos/pOb0pjXpn6Q).

## The Path Forward

The Observer's transition requires exactly one thing: a real task, done today. Not a tutorial. Not a course. One actual deliverable from your to-do list, run through Claude or ChatGPT, refined until it's good. The [ask-review-pushback-repeat loop](/essays/2026-03-13-01-start-with-ai-today) is the only skill that matters at this stage.

## Level Up

### Mindset Shift

From "waiting for permission/clarity" to "learning by doing, one task at a time." Stop treating AI as something to understand before using — treat it as something you understand *by* using.

### Skill Milestones

- Complete one real work task with AI daily for a week (not tutorials — actual deliverables)
- Experience the ask-review-pushback-repeat loop at least 10 times
- Recognize when AI output needs pushback (even if you can't always fix it yet)
- Stop asking "are we allowed to use this?" and start asking "how do I use this better?"
