Rejection competency is the skill of knowing when to say "no" to AI output. As AI-generated content becomes increasingly fluent and superficially convincing, the ability to distinguish between output that is genuinely correct and output that merely looks right becomes a critical professional skill.

Most AI users accept the first reasonable-looking output, but experienced practitioners know that the difference between "good enough" and "actually correct" is where real value lies. Rejection competency requires domain expertise, critical thinking, and the discipline to iterate rather than ship. It pairs with [specification quality](/knowledge/specification-quality) — the better your spec, the easier it is to evaluate the output against it. The [Displaced Expert](/personas/05-displaced-expert) has a natural advantage in rejection competency — they know what 'wrong' looks like because they've spent years building that intuition. Their domain knowledge becomes a filter that AI users without deep expertise cannot replicate.

## Key Aspects

- **Domain knowledge as filter** — you can only reject bad output if you understand the domain well enough to spot errors
- **The 83% trap** — AI output is often 83% correct, which is enough to look convincing but not enough to be reliable; the METR study found experienced developers were 19% slower with AI while *believing* they were 24% faster, precisely because they accepted output that looked right
- **Iteration discipline** — rejection competency means being willing to run multiple rounds rather than accepting the first pass
- **[Frontier recognition](/knowledge/frontier-recognition)** — knowing when you've hit the limits of what AI can do and human expertise must take over
