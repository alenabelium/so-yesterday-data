AI-native economics describes the fundamentally different financial model of companies built around AI from the ground up. These companies achieve revenue-per-employee ratios 5-7x above even elite traditional tech companies: Cursor at $5M per employee, Lovable at $13M, and teams of 45 people generating $200M in revenue. This isn't incremental improvement — it's a structural shift in how companies create and capture value.

The shift works because AI collapses execution costs toward zero. When agents write code, handle support, generate marketing, and manage data pipelines, the human contribution concentrates on judgment: what to build, for whom, and why. The [Solo Operator](/knowledge/solo-operator) model is the extreme expression — one person, AI-amplified, operating at company scale. But even larger AI-native teams operate with radically different economics than their traditional peers.

This creates a paradox for established companies: AI makes them more productive, but it also enables tiny competitors to match their output. The SaaS market sell-off of early 2026 — Thomson Reuters down 16%, LegalZoom down 20% — was the market pricing in this reality. Per-seat licensing models crack when a 200-line prompt replicates functionality that took a company years to build.

## Key Aspects

- **Revenue-per-employee ratios** — AI-native companies run 5-7x above traditional benchmarks, with some approaching $5-13M per employee
- **Execution cost collapse** — as AI handles implementation, the cost of producing software, content, and analysis approaches zero; value concentrates in judgment and distribution
- **SaaS model disruption** — per-seat licensing under pressure when AI can replicate software functionality; Anthropic's Claude Co-work plugins triggered market sell-off
- **Minimum viable team** — the minimum team size for a competitive product has shrunk dramatically; solo founders now represent a third of new US ventures
