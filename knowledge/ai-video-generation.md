AI video generation encompasses tools and models that create, edit, and transform video content from text prompts, reference images, or other video inputs. The field has exploded in capability since late 2025, with commercial platforms like Kling, Seedance, and the now-discontinued Sora, alongside open-source models like LTX, competing to deliver increasingly realistic and controllable output. Kling 3.0's multi-shot feature -- allowing users to define individual shots with separate prompts, durations, and camera directions stitched into coherent 15-second cinematic sequences -- represents the current state of the art for consumer-accessible video generation.

The technology sits at the intersection of [multimodal AI](/knowledge/multimodal-ai) and generative models, leveraging diffusion-based architectures trained on massive video datasets. Key capabilities now include character consistency across shots, natural-language video editing with reference images, multilingual dialogue generation, and physically accurate motion for complex movements like breakdancing and fight sequences. Open-source alternatives like LTX have reached the point of running locally with sound generation, 4K output, and low VRAM requirements, democratizing access beyond cloud-only commercial platforms. The pace of iteration is extreme -- models that were state-of-the-art three months ago are routinely surpassed.

The competitive dynamics are notable. OpenAI shut down Sora after hemorrhaging compute costs, redirecting resources to its core language model efforts. Chinese labs (Kling from Kuaishou, Seedance from ByteDance) have taken the lead in video generation quality, while open-source projects enable local generation. The commercial model remains unsettled: video generation is compute-intensive and the willingness-to-pay for AI-generated video is still being established. Hollywood and creative industries are watching closely as the gap between AI-generated and professionally produced video narrows.

## Key Aspects

- **Multi-shot generation** -- Kling 3.0 pioneered defining individual shots with separate prompts, durations, and camera directions that maintain character consistency across an entire sequence
- **Natural-language editing** -- models like Kling's Video 3 Omni enable reference-based video editing using plain language, combining reference images and style directions without manual compositing
- **Open-source progress** -- LTX and similar projects bring video generation to local hardware with sound, 4K resolution, and manageable VRAM requirements
- **Competitive landscape** -- Chinese labs lead in quality, OpenAI exited via Sora shutdown, and the commercial model for AI video remains unsettled
