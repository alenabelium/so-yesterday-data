The three layers of work is a framework for understanding which aspects of jobs are most affected by AI. It divides professional work into three layers: execution (doing the task), judgment (deciding how to do it), and strategy (deciding what to do). AI is rapidly automating the execution layer, augmenting the judgment layer, and leaving the strategy layer largely to humans — for now. The essay 'The Last Hands on the Keyboard' maps these layers as Implementation (code writing, now handled by agents), Architecture (system design, now shared between humans and AI), and Intent (what to build and why, entirely human). This maps directly to the persona progression: [The Builder](/personas/03-builder) works across all three layers, [The Orchestrator](/personas/04-orchestrator) concentrates on judgment and strategy, and [The Solo Operator](/personas/07-solo-operator) operates primarily at the strategy layer with AI handling execution and augmenting judgment.

This framework is more useful than simple "will AI take my job?" analyses because most jobs contain all three layers in different proportions. A developer writes code (execution), chooses architectures (judgment), and decides what to build (strategy). AI changes the proportions: as execution is automated, professionals who operate primarily at the judgment and strategy layers become more valuable. This connects directly to [agent orchestration](/knowledge/agent-orchestration) — the skill of operating at the judgment layer while agents handle execution.

## Key Aspects

- **Execution layer** — the task itself (writing code, drafting documents, analyzing data) — most automatable
- **Judgment layer** — quality assessment, tradeoff decisions, exception handling — augmented by AI but requiring human oversight
- **Strategy layer** — goal setting, prioritization, stakeholder alignment — still primarily human
- **Layer shifting** — AI doesn't eliminate jobs; it shifts the proportion of time spent at each layer
