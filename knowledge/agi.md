Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) refers to AI systems that can match or exceed human-level performance across the full range of cognitive tasks — not just narrow specialties like chess or code generation, but reasoning, creativity, planning, and learning in any domain. Whether current systems are approaching AGI, and what it would mean if they did, is one of the most contested questions in the field.

AGI functions as both a technical goal and a cultural marker. [Frontier labs](/knowledge/frontier-labs) orient their research roadmaps around it, investors price it into valuations, and careers are shaped by beliefs about its timeline. The debate matters because AGI-level systems would fundamentally alter the economic and social landscape — making questions of [alignment](/knowledge/alignment) and safety existential rather than merely important.

## Key Aspects

- **Moving goalposts** — each time AI achieves a previously "AGI-level" capability (passing bar exams, writing code, scientific reasoning), the definition shifts
- **Timeline disagreement** — estimates range from 2-3 years (optimists) to decades or never (skeptics); Ray Kurzweil predicts 2029
- **[Emergent behavior](/knowledge/emergent-behavior)** as evidence — unexpected capabilities appearing at scale are cited both as evidence for imminent AGI and as evidence that we don't understand what we're building
- **[Scaling laws](/knowledge/scaling-laws)** as roadmap — the predictable improvement of models with more compute fuels AGI optimism, though whether scaling alone is sufficient is debated
