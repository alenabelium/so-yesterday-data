The 70% problem is the observation that AI tools consistently get to roughly 70% quality on most tasks very quickly — a first draft, a working prototype, a plausible analysis — but closing the gap from 70% to 95% (production-quality, reliable, nuanced) requires genuine human skill. Most people either accept the 70% output (creating a flood of mediocre work) or don't know how to bridge the gap efficiently.

This is the central challenge of the [Practitioner](/personas/02-practitioner) persona. The first week with AI is exhilarating — everything is faster. The second and third weeks reveal the 70% ceiling: output that "looks right" but contains subtle errors, missing edge cases, or surface-level reasoning. Those who push past this develop [rejection competency](/knowledge/rejection-competency) and [specification quality](/knowledge/specification-quality) — the meta-skills for closing the gap. Those who don't either accept mediocre output or fall off the [three-week cliff](/knowledge/three-week-cliff).

The 70% problem also explains why experienced professionals sometimes perform worse with AI initially. The METR study found that experienced developers were 19% slower with AI on certain tasks while believing they were 24% faster — they accepted 70% output that introduced bugs their manual process would have caught.

## Key Aspects

- **Speed trap** — the 70% arrives so fast that it creates pressure to ship before the critical last 30% is done
- **The "looks right" bias** — AI output is fluent and well-formatted, making errors harder to spot than in rough human drafts
- **Calibration as the skill** — knowing where in your domain AI reliably hits 95% vs. where it consistently tops out at 70% is [frontier recognition](/knowledge/frontier-recognition) in practice
- **Iterative closing** — the gap is closed through cycles of specification, generation, evaluation, and refinement — not by accepting the first pass
