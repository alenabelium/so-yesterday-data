Frontier recognition is the skill of accurately understanding where the boundary of AI capability lies — what AI can do reliably, what it can do with human oversight, and what it cannot yet do at all. This boundary shifts rapidly, making frontier recognition an ongoing practice rather than a one-time assessment.

People who lack frontier recognition fall into two traps: underestimating AI (missing opportunities for automation and augmentation) or overestimating it (trusting AI with tasks where it fails silently). Both are costly. The [compounding gap](/knowledge/compounding-gap) between those who track the frontier and those who don't grows wider each month. The Bicycle Problem essay identifies frontier recognition as one of six judgment skills that survive AI disruption, alongside context assembly, quality judgment, task decomposition, iterative refinement, and [rejection competency](/knowledge/rejection-competency). The [Practitioner](/personas/02-practitioner) develops frontier recognition through daily calibration — learning where in their domain AI reliably performs at 95% and where it consistently tops out at 70%.

## Key Aspects

- **Rapid obsolescence** — what was impossible six months ago may be routine today; frontier recognition requires continuous learning
- **Capability vs. reliability** — AI may be capable of a task in demos but unreliable in production; knowing the difference matters
- **Strategic application** — frontier recognition informs where to invest in AI tooling and where to maintain human processes
- **Skill pairing** — works alongside [rejection competency](/knowledge/rejection-competency) to form a complete evaluation framework
