Synthetic data is training data generated by AI models rather than collected from real-world sources. As frontier models exhaust available high-quality human-generated data, synthetic data has become a critical technique for continued improvement. Models generate training examples, reasoning traces, or domain-specific datasets that are then filtered for quality and used to train the next generation of models or [fine-tune](/knowledge/fine-tuning) existing ones for specific tasks. This approach addresses multiple constraints simultaneously: data scarcity in specialized domains, privacy regulations that restrict use of real data, and the sheer cost of human annotation at scale.

The technique is deeply intertwined with [scaling laws](/knowledge/scaling-laws). The original scaling paradigm assumed performance improves with more compute, parameters, and data -- but high-quality human data is finite. Synthetic data extends the data axis by generating targeted training examples that fill gaps in existing datasets. The mathematics distillation challenge launched by Terry Tao exemplifies this approach: compressing complex reasoning into compact "cheat sheets" that boost LLM performance on mathematical tasks, essentially using AI-generated summaries as training data for other AI systems. Similarly, model [distillation](/knowledge/model-distillation) relies on synthetic data when a large teacher model generates outputs that a smaller student model learns to replicate.

The risks are real. Training on AI-generated data can amplify biases, create feedback loops where model errors compound across generations, and produce "model collapse" where diversity degrades over successive training rounds. Quality filtering -- separating useful synthetic examples from noise -- has become its own research area. Despite these challenges, synthetic data is now standard practice at every [frontier lab](/knowledge/frontier-labs) and is particularly valuable for [open-source AI](/knowledge/open-source-ai) projects that lack access to the massive proprietary datasets held by large companies.

## Key Aspects

- **Self-improvement loops** -- models generate training data for their own successors, enabling recursive improvement but requiring careful quality filtering to avoid compounding errors
- **Domain-specific generation** -- synthetic data is especially valuable for specialized domains (medical, legal, scientific) where real-world data is scarce, sensitive, or expensive to annotate
- **Privacy compliance** -- synthetic data sidesteps privacy regulations by generating statistically similar but non-identifiable training examples, critical for healthcare, finance, and enterprise AI
- **Quality filtering** -- the central challenge is ensuring synthetic data improves rather than degrades model performance; techniques include AI-based scoring, human spot-checking, and benchmark validation
