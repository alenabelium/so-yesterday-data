Post-labor economics is the economic framework, theorized extensively by David Shapiro, for understanding and navigating a world where AI and robotics decouple economic output from human labor. The core thesis is that all household income flows from three buckets -- wages (currently 82%), transfers (13%), and capital (5%) -- and as automation makes human labor structurally redundant, the ratio must flip so that capital and transfers provide the majority of household income. Without this shift, the economy enters a deflationary death spiral: firms cut workers, household spending collapses, and demand craters even as production capacity soars.

The framework draws on historical precedent. The primary sector (farming, mining) fell from employing 90% of the population to under 2%. The secondary sector (manufacturing) hollowed out industrial cities across the developed world. Shapiro argues the tertiary sector (services) peaked around 2015 and is now following the same trajectory, driven by AI's ability to perform cognitive and even empathetic tasks. The "Assumption of the Indispensability of Labor" -- the unquestioned premise that human effort is structurally required for the world to function -- is collapsing on both economic and philosophical fronts, forcing what Shapiro calls humanity's graduation from agents to principals.

The practical implications are immediate. Individuals are advised to transition from wage dependency to capital participation through dividend-producing assets, ESOPs, and location arbitrage. At the policy level, the framework calls for [sovereign wealth funds](/knowledge/sovereign-wealth-funds), baby bonds, auto-enrolled investment accounts, and expanded transfer programs to bridge the gap while capital-based income mechanisms mature. The uncomfortable math: sovereign wealth funds take 25-30 years to reach maturity, while the automation cliff arrives in the 2030s.

## Key Aspects

- **Three income buckets** -- wages, transfers, and capital are the only sources of household income; post-labor economics focuses on systematically growing the latter two as wages decline
- **Historical sector decline** -- farming, manufacturing, and now services each follow the same peak-and-decline pattern; the service sector's decline is accelerated by AI's cognitive capabilities
- **The reinstatement effect breakdown** -- historically, automation destroyed old jobs but created new human-advantage tasks; AI threatens this dynamic for the first time by matching humans across strength, dexterity, cognition, and empathy
- **Ontological shock** -- beyond economics, post-labor economics grapples with the identity crisis of a species that has defined meaning through work for millennia ([Identity Threat](/knowledge/identity-threat))
