# Apple Just Positioned Itself for the Next Trillion Dollars

## Executive Summary

Nate Jones argues that Tim Cook's departure and the appointment of hardware engineer John Turnis as Apple CEO signals a deliberate strategic pivot: rather than trying to win the cloud AI race Apple was losing, the company is betting on on-device AI powered by Apple Silicon. He explains that cloud AI economics are structurally unprofitable at consumer scale (even OpenAI loses money on ChatGPT Pro at $200/month), while on-device inference has fixed costs after the hardware purchase. Jones identifies a massive unserved market in regulated professional services (law firms, medical practices, financial advisers) who need AI but cannot use the cloud for compliance reasons, and frames Apple's move as a repeat of the Apple II strategy that moved useful computing off metered mainframes onto owned devices 50 years ago.

## Key Points

- Apple's new CEO and chief hardware officer are both silicon engineers, signaling Apple is abandoning the software velocity AI race it was structurally losing and betting instead on on-device AI where the marginal cost of inference drops to near zero after hardware purchase. [00:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RaAFquzj5B8&t=0)
- Cloud AI economics are upside down: frontier labs lose money on consumer subscriptions, GPU supply is constrained by power and fab capacity, and per-token prices are falling slower than frontier capabilities scale -- pointing toward a two-class AI system where enterprises get real AI and consumers get metered access. [04:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RaAFquzj5B8&t=287)
- A trillion-dollar professional services segment (law firms, medical practices, financial advisers) has a structural need for AI that never touches the cloud due to attorney-client privilege, HIPAA, and fiduciary duties -- many are already improvising with clusters of Mac Minis, but no vendor has built the enterprise stack they need. [11:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RaAFquzj5B8&t=666)
