# OpenAI Just Gave Every Team A Free Employee. Here's The Catch.

## Executive Summary

Nate Jones analyzes ChatGPT Workspace Agents, arguing they represent a direct competitor to lightweight automation platforms like Zapier and Make rather than just an upgrade to custom GPTs. Based on his observations of teams using the research preview, workspace agents succeed on recurring workflows that cross two or three tools, have clear good/bad outputs, and can be described in a paragraph. He walks through concrete first-build examples for sales, product, customer success, and coordination roles, emphasizes that the enterprise governance model (role-based controls, version history, compliance APIs) is what will actually win enterprise seats, and warns that the biggest failure mode is pointing agents at novel, judgment-heavy work instead of repeatable processes where the path is known.

## Key Points

- Workspace agents powered by Codex in the cloud shift from "the tool helps me do the work" to "the tool does a first pass and I review" -- an RFP workflow that formerly took hours of assembling now takes 20 minutes of editing, with the agent pulling prior responses from SharePoint and flagging gaps. [04:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QrvVkm-8Jx4&t=290)
- The governance layer is the actual product for enterprises: admins control who builds, publishes, and runs agents, which apps are allowed, what actions require approval, and role-based controls prevent creators' personal app connections from inadvertently giving all users access to sensitive systems. [13:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QrvVkm-8Jx4&t=826)
- Workspace agents compete most directly with the lightweight automation layer (Zapier, Make, N8N, Copilot Studio), and the strategic read is that OpenAI wants to turn Codex and workspace agents into the default operating system for all corporate work -- contrasting with Anthropic's more vertical product strategy. [18:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QrvVkm-8Jx4&t=1132)
