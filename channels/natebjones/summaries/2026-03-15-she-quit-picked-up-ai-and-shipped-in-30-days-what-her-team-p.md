# She quit, picked up AI, and shipped in 30 days what her team planned for Q3.

## Executive Summary

Nate Jones argues that the surge in AI-powered solo founders — now a third of all new US ventures according to Carta — reveals that extraordinary people were never blocked by ability, they were blocked by overhead. Drawing on examples like Ben Sira ($2.5M ARR solo with Pulsia) and Sarah Gilliam (profiled by the Economist as a potential one-person unicorn), he identifies the real skills separating extraordinary AI performers from everyone else: not tool fluency, but conviction (the willingness to act before validation), speed of control (the ability to triage and make high-quality sequential decisions), and judgment density. A Harvard Business School experiment at P&G found that individuals with AI were 3x more likely to produce top-10% quality ideas, with AI breaking down functional silos. The uncomfortable implication for companies: if leaders don't remove coordination overhead and give their best people space to act boldly, those people will simply leave and solo found.

## Key Points

- The critical distinction is between taste (evaluating what's good) and conviction (the willingness to act on that evaluation before anyone else confirms it) — extraordinary AI performers run a feedback loop between the two, and two years of AI-native building may now produce more relevant pattern recognition than eight years of traditional execution.
- Speed of control matters more than span of control: managing dozens of AI agents isn't about holding everything in your head, it's about how fast you can make high-quality triage decisions — an executive-level skill that is now required at the individual contributor level.
- Solo founding is rising sharply because AI removes the overhead that always blocked extraordinary people, and companies face a talent retention crisis unless they strip out coordination burden, kill averaging-by-committee, and empower individuals to disagree and commit at scale.
