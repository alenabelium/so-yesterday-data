# The $285 Billion Crash Wall Street Won't Explain Honestly. Here's What Everyone Missed.

## Executive Summary

Anthropic's release of open-source Claude Co-work plugins -- including a 200-line legal contract review prompt -- triggered a $285 billion sell-off in enterprise SaaS stocks, with Thomson Reuters down 16%, RELX down 14%, and LegalZoom cratering 20%. The markdown file did not cause the crash; it revealed that the per-seat SaaS licensing model was already cracking. The more consequential story was KPMG using AI as negotiating leverage to force a 14% fee reduction from its auditor Grant Thornton -- a real operating event that previews how AI will cascade through every professional services fee negotiation, not by automating work but by changing the economics everyone can see.

## Key Points

- Jensen Huang's counterargument that "AI runs on software so software benefits" defends the product but misses the attack on the pricing model: if one AI agent does research that previously required 10 paralegals with 10 Westlaw logins, Thomson Reuters loses nine seats of revenue while its data becomes more important, not less
- The SaaS survival path requires pivoting from UI-first to agentic-first architecture (charging for data value and accountability rather than per-seat access) while simultaneously maintaining the legacy product -- a transformation that tends to kill companies that attempt it too slowly
- As building software approaches zero cost, the buy-vs-build calculus flips for the first time: a custom CRM built by AI in an afternoon challenges the premise that it is cheaper to buy general-purpose tools, though the "articulation problem" (extracting the 95% of implicit requirements) remains a real bottleneck
