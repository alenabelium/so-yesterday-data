# Sora Was Burning $15 Million a Day. That's Not Even the Scary Part.

## Executive Summary

Nate Jones reads under the March 2026 headlines to identify five structural shifts that will shape the AI industry over the next 12 months. Sora's shutdown after burning $15 million per day in inference costs signals the industry is hitting an inference wall, not a training wall, making cost-per-delivered-unit-of-revenue the new critical metric. The first real ad dollars entered conversational AI interfaces with 1.5x conversion rates, threatening Google's $300 billion search advertising model. Meanwhile, US data center construction faces a three-layer contradiction between federal deregulation, state/local moratoria in 12+ states, and Gulf conflict disrupting Middle Eastern sites. The SaaS business model is in crisis as per-seat pricing collapses, and Anthropic's defense department standoff reveals that safety posture has become a market positioning question with direct revenue consequences.

## Key Points

- Sora was burning an estimated $15 million per day in inference costs against just $2.1 million in lifetime revenue before OpenAI shut it down, marking the loudest signal yet that the hard constraint in AI has shifted from training to inference -- and the most important metric is now inference cost per delivered unit of revenue, not training flop count. [01:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0vdlwOK_Qdk&t=117)
- The first ad tech integration with ChatGPT through Criteo showed users converting at 1.5x the rate of other referral channels, and since discovery, consideration, and conversion now happen in a single conversation context window, this represents the first surface in a decade that credibly threatens Google's core $300 billion advertising monetization model. [04:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0vdlwOK_Qdk&t=271)
- Anthropic's refusal to allow Claude for fully autonomous weapons or mass surveillance cost a $200 million Pentagon contract and triggered a government-wide ban, but it also drove record consumer adoption and enterprise goodwill -- demonstrating that safety posture is no longer just an ethics question but a market positioning decision with revenue consequences running in multiple directions.
