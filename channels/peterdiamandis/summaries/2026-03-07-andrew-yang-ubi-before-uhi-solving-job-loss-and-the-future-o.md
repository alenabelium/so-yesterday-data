# Andrew Yang: UBI Before UHI, Solving Job Loss, and the Future of Work 

## Executive Summary

Andrew Yang joins the Moonshots panel to discuss the path from universal basic income to universal high income in the context of accelerating AI-driven job displacement. Yang argues that UBI must come before Elon Musk's vision of UHI, since the logistics of wealth redistribution through government are too slow and the social contract is already disintegrating -- recent college grad unemployment exceeds 50%, home ownership average age is 40, and public anger is rising across the political spectrum. The conversation explores two pathways to UBI: government action (deemed unlikely) and billionaire philanthropy modeled on Michael Dell's geographically-centered giving. Yang also outlines a potential 2028 third-party presidential run leveraging Elon's media platform, popular discontent with both parties (50% of Americans identify as independent), and an innovative smartphone-based primary process.

## Key Points

- Yang contends UBI must precede UHI because job loss is a 2026-2027 crisis requiring immediate cash transfers, while supply-side solutions like abundant housing and AI healthcare will take years to build; he advocates starting around $2,000/month per person, funded initially by billionaire philanthropy rather than government. [23:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=toE56X2h0wk&t=1422)
- The social contract is actively disintegrating: the CEO of a healthcare company was shot and his killer lionized, young people increasingly view successful people as villains, and DC operates on a "multi-decade tape delay" that has become catastrophic given AI's acceleration -- making rampant social unrest likely within 2-3 years without intervention. [00:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=toE56X2h0wk&t=0)
- A viable 2028 third-party presidential bid is possible because all three prerequisites exist: capital (billionaire backing), a media megaphone (Elon owns X), and popular movement (50% identify as independent, both parties below 32% approval), with the potential for a smartphone-based independent primary featuring candidates like Mark Cuban and Oprah.
