# Davos 2026: The US-China AI Race, GPU Diplomacy, and Robots Walking the Streets 

## Executive Summary

Diamandis and his co-hosts recap the major AI conversations from Davos 2026, where the World Economic Forum was dominated entirely by AI for the first time. The episode covers the US-China AI race, GPU export diplomacy, humanoid robots appearing in public settings, and the debate over whether AI development should be slowed down. Key voices from Davos, including AI lab leaders and policymakers, weigh in on timelines for AGI, the geopolitical implications of compute access, and the challenge of ensuring safety while maintaining competitive advantage. The panel notes that while the US leads in model quality and chip design, China has advantages in energy generation speed and manufacturing capacity, making the race far from decided.

## Key Points

- Davos 2026 was entirely dominated by AI discussions, a stark shift from previous years focused on politics and economics; the consensus among attendees was that AGI capabilities are imminent, with some leaders suggesting a slightly slower pace would help get safety right.
- The US-China AI race is more nuanced than commonly understood: the US leads in model quality and chip design, but China is spinning up power generation and manufacturing faster, and GPU export controls have become a central tool of diplomatic leverage with complex geopolitical implications.
- Humanoid robots walking the streets at Davos represented a visceral demonstration of how quickly AI is moving from digital to physical domains, reinforcing the panel's thesis that trillions in data center capex will literally march out the door in robotic form.
