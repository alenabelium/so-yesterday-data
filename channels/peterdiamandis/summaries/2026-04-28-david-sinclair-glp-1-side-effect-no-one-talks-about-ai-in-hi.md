# David Sinclair: GLP-1 Side Effect No One Talks About, AI in His Lab & Reversing Blindness | EP #251

## Executive Summary

Harvard geneticist David Sinclair joins Peter Diamandis for a Q&A session covering GLP-1 drugs, AI in longevity research, and reversing age-related diseases. Sinclair warns about an underreported GLP-1 side effect -- a rare but serious risk of blindness (NAION) affecting 20,000-30,000 people annually in the US. He describes how AI is exponentially accelerating his lab's work, including AI-driven drug design scanning billions of molecules for aging-reversal candidates and an agentic AI system called Cadence that independently made novel discoveries in longevity data. The conversation also covers OSK gene therapy showing promise in reversing ALS, macular degeneration, and glaucoma in animal models, and the debate over whether genetics or lifestyle matters more for lifespan.

## Key Points

- GLP-1 drugs show broad benefits for heart and brain beyond weight loss, but Sinclair highlights an underreported risk: approximately 20,000-30,000 people per year in the US are experiencing blindness (NAION) as a side effect, which his lab's ER100 therapy may eventually be able to reverse. [03:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8ohmtB8MdI&t=192)
- AI is transforming Sinclair's lab: they're using AI-driven drug design to scan billions of molecules for aging-reversal candidates, AI to classify cell age from microscopy in minutes, and an agentic AI system called Cadence that autonomously analyzed transcriptomic data and made a novel discovery about biological age that the team hadn't thought of. [07:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8ohmtB8MdI&t=431)
- OSK gene therapy (ER100) is showing surprisingly broad results in animal models, with Kelly Rich demonstrating reversible regrowth of motor neurons in ALS and multiple labs showing reversal of both wet and dry macular degeneration -- suggesting these degenerative conditions may not be permanently progressive as previously assumed.
