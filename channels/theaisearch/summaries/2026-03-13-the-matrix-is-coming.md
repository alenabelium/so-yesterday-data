# The Matrix is coming

## Executive Summary

Cortical Labs built CL1, a chip powered by 200,000 living human brain cells that successfully learned to play the video game Doom. The system uses a micro-electrode array where neurons physically touch silicon, enabling two-way communication -- the chip stimulates neurons with game state data and reads their firing patterns back as game controls. Training leverages the "free energy principle": correct actions receive smooth electrical signals while mistakes trigger chaotic noise, causing neurons to rewire themselves to improve. This breakthrough matters beyond gaming because biological neural chips learn far faster than traditional AI, consume dramatically less energy (the human brain runs on just 20 watts), and could excel at real-world robotics tasks where silicon AI struggles.

## Key Points

- The CL1 chip keeps 200,000 living human neurons alive for up to 6 months using a self-contained life-support system with nutrient delivery, waste filtration, temperature regulation at 37 degrees C, and gas control -- all fitting in a standard server rack form factor. [03:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZqRtR6Z2U6U&t=222)
- Training uses the free energy principle: neurons prefer predictable environments, so correct game actions receive smooth electrical signals while mistakes trigger chaotic noise, causing the biological network to rewire itself and learn Doom gameplay within hours rather than the millions of iterations traditional AI requires. [14:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZqRtR6Z2U6U&t=889)
- These biological chips could solve AI's energy crisis (GPT-3 training used 1,300 MWh while the human brain runs on 20 watts) and address the Moravec paradox -- tasks easy for humans like physical manipulation are extremely hard for silicon AI but natural for biological neural networks. [17:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZqRtR6Z2U6U&t=1027)
