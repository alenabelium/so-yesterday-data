# “I like being scared”: Molly Graham’s frameworks for rapid career growth 

## Executive Summary

Molly Graham, who held early roles at Google, Facebook, Quip, and the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative, shares her greatest-hits collection of leadership frameworks for thriving inside fast-growing companies. The conversation centers on her famous “give away your Legos” advice -- the idea that career growth in scaling companies requires repeatedly handing off responsibilities you’ve mastered to take on new, unfamiliar challenges. She also introduces “Bob,” an externalized mental model for the destructive emotions that accompany organizational change, and a two-week rule for distinguishing temporary emotional reactions from real problems worth escalating.

## Key Points

- The “give away your Legos” framework: in rapidly scaling companies, your job grows as fast as the business, so you must constantly hand off work you’ve gotten good at to new hires and move on to unfamiliar challenges. Holding on to your current responsibilities means getting buried under a pile of Legos rather than staying on top of the growing opportunity. [11:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=twzLDx9iers&t=681)
- Graham externalizes the negative emotions that accompany change into a character she calls “Bob” -- a monster whose job is to make you the worst version of yourself. The key insight is that these territorial and fearful reactions are normal but not useful; give them two weeks, and if the feeling persists beyond that, it signals a real issue worth addressing with a manager or coach. [17:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=twzLDx9iers&t=1027)
- The J-curve versus stairs model of career growth: traditional careers look like stairs (steady promotions every two years), but the most rewarding careers look like jumping off cliffs -- you fall initially but then climb out far beyond where the stairs could ever reach, which is why Graham advises seeking roles you feel highly unqualified for.
