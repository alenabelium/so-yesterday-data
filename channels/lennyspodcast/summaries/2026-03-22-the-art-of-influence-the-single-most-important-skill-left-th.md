# The art of influence: The single most important skill left that AI can’t replace 

## Executive Summary

Jessica Feain, a product leader who has held roles at Box, Slack, and Webflow, argues that as AI automates execution, the ability to influence executives and stakeholders becomes the single highest-leverage skill for product leaders. She shares tactical advice including understanding executive calendars and incentive structures, going into meetings to learn rather than convince, and presenting multiple options to build trust. The conversation explores how AI is ushering in a "golden age of product management" where the core skills shift from project coordination to deciding what to build and rallying people behind those ideas. Jessica also discusses how agents can be treated like junior team members that need onboarding with clear product principles and guardrails.

## Key Points

- Executives operate under constant context-switching ("strobe light calendars"), so the most effective tactic is spending 30-60 seconds at the top of every meeting resetting context -- why you’re there, what happened last time, and what the goals are -- then asking if there’s anything else they want to cover. [07:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RP4vJeIb7WU&t=441)
- As execution complexity plummets and everyone becomes a builder, the leverage shifts from being the best note-taker or Gantt chart master to deciding what work survives and influencing others to fund it; AI tools like Claude can now simulate executive pushback on PRDs, letting PMs pre-test their pitches before the real meeting. [20:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RP4vJeIb7WU&t=1215)
- With agents joining as AI teammates, product leaders must codify their product philosophy, success criteria, and guardrails into documentation -- essentially onboarding agents the same way you would onboard junior employees -- so that the agents can operate autonomously while reflecting the team’s taste and judgment.
