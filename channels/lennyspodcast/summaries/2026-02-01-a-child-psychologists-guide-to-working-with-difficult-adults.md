# A child psychologist’s guide to working with difficult adults 

## Executive Summary

Dr. Becky Kennedy, clinical psychologist and founder of Good Inside, bridges the gap between parenting advice and workplace leadership. Her core thesis is that the same principles that help parents deal with difficult children apply directly to managing difficult adults at work, because all humans -- whether one year old or 45 -- need the same things and express themselves in similar ineffective ways when those needs are unmet. The conversation covers the power of repair (going back after a moment you are not proud of), connecting before correcting, separating identity from behavior ("good inside" framework), and the phrase "we’re on the same team" as the single most productive opener for any difficult conversation.

## Key Points

- Repair is the number one relationship strategy: secure attachment is not defined by getting it right all the time but by the willingness to go back after a mistake and take responsibility. At work, this looks like saying "I cut you off in the meeting, I used a harsh tone, that wasn’t okay, and I’m working on it" -- nothing reestablishes trust and cooperation faster. [09:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Auxs8ZsHRI4&t=558)
- The "good inside" principle requires separating identity from behavior: instead of "that person is lazy" (collapsing behavior and identity), say "this is a good person who is late." Starting difficult conversations with "we’re on the same team" is the single most effective way to prevent defensiveness and have a productive conversation, whether with a child or a direct report. [18:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Auxs8ZsHRI4&t=1088)
- Connecting before correcting: the mindset matters more than the script. Genuine connection means being present without an agenda, even for 30 seconds -- sitting next to someone and saying "I’m happy to see you" or asking about their weekend without counting down the seconds to discuss work. This forms a bridge between two people that enables cooperation and productivity, and efficiency-oriented people must deliberately practice dropping into "relationship mode."
