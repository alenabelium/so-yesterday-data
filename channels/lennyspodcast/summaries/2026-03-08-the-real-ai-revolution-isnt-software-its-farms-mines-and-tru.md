# The real AI revolution isn't software. It's farms, mines, and trucks. 

## Executive Summary

Caster Ununice, co-founder and CEO of Applied Intuition (a $15 billion physical AI company), argues that the real AI revolution over the next 5-10 years will happen not in software but in farming, mining, construction, and trucking -- industries where the average worker age is in the late 50s and labor shortages are already critical. Applied Intuition adds AI to existing vehicles and physical machines (serving 18 of top 20 automakers plus defense), essentially providing the Waymo/Tesla software layer without the hardware. Caster draws parallels to the industrial revolution and the pre-iPhone mobile era to argue that intelligence added to already-existing physical machines will have far greater societal impact than AI tools used by the small percentage of people in the software bubble.

## Key Points

- The real impact of AI in the next 5-10 years will be in farming, mining, construction, and trucking where workers are aging out and nobody wants the dangerous jobs; the average farmer is 58 years old, and in 10-20 years these industries face catastrophic labor shortages that AI autonomy is uniquely positioned to solve. [00:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_rcniEb9bLw&t=0)
- Caster uses the pre-iPhone mobile analogy: just as we could not have predicted Instagram in 2006 before app stores and dual cameras existed, we cannot fully imagine what applications will emerge once every car and industrial machine has embedded intelligence -- but the most pragmatic near-term path is adding intelligence to machines that already exist rather than building humanoid robots. [20:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_rcniEb9bLw&t=1258)
- He pushes back on China anxiety by noting that Chinese companies like Huawei are extensions of the state, not comparable to American companies competing in free markets; he warns against pumping the brakes on frontier technology, arguing that slowing innovation actually hurts the most marginalized workers the most since American economic growth overwhelmingly comes from the technology sector.
