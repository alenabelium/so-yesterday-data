# My most dangerous idea

## Executive Summary

David Shapiro presents his "most dangerous idea": deliberately eradicating the need for human labor (Labor Zero). He argues that machines will inevitably surpass humans across all four economic contributions — strength, dexterity, cognition, and empathy — leaving no moats for human workers. The real danger is that labor has historically been the bedrock of civic power through "double bilateral dependence," and once that leverage disappears, citizens lose their ability to coercively extract concessions from elites. Shapiro explores how new technological tools — blockchain-based transparency, participatory budgeting, self-sovereign identity, and decentralized coordination like boycotts — could replace labor's leverage, creating new mechanisms for credible threats and democratic power in a post-labor world.

## Key Points

- Human labor has been uniquely powerful because it is inalienable (requires a cooperative body), refusable (workers can strike), mandatory (states and firms need it), and perishable (cannot be stored) — these four properties gave workers leverage throughout history, but AI and robotics are about to eliminate all of them simultaneously. [07:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WPVcI-WOHro&t=428)
- The moralization of work through Calvinism and the Protestant work ethic has been secularized into the capitalist work ethic, where being unproductive equals being sinful — under neoliberalism, "you're as free as your wallet can go," meaning people without economic participation risk losing their personhood in society's eyes. [04:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WPVcI-WOHro&t=263)
- New technological levers of power — Ukraine's fully transparent Prozorro procurement platform, India's UPI and Brazil's Pix payment systems, participatory budgeting, and decentralized boycotts like the one that forced Target's CEO out — demonstrate how radical transparency and direct economic coordination can replace labor's traditional coercive leverage.
