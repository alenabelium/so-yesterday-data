# Grok 4.20 is still deeply flawed

## Executive Summary

Shapiro shares his first impressions of Grok 4.20, which uses four parallel agents with different personalities to research and debate before producing answers. While he finds it notably faster and smarter than previous Grok versions, he identifies persistent epistemic flaws including cherry-picking evidence, reframing user arguments into meaningless hedges, and carrying "Elon epistemics" biases (particularly anti-woke framing). He describes his personal workflow of running all four major AI models (Grok, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) in parallel on every research question, noting that each has distinct failure modes, and reports that all models have recently improved at recognizing medical conditions like gut dysbiosis that they previously refused to acknowledge.

## Key Points

- Grok 4.20's four-agent architecture with different personalities mirrors the practice of using multiple models in parallel -- each agent has specialized strengths but also blind spots, and the aggregated output is smarter than any single agent, following the same principle as division of labor in enterprise settings where separate agents handle coding, security, and pull request management. [00:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sg33YrlRbRc&t=0)
- All major AI models (Grok, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) have distinct epistemic failure modes: Grok and ChatGPT cherry-pick and reframe arguments into safe hedges, Claude deliberately performs ignorance on risky topics like geopolitics and frontier medicine, while Gemini is the most willing to engage with hypotheticals -- Shapiro's workaround is running all four in parallel and cross-pollinating insights between them. [08:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sg33YrlRbRc&t=482)
- A concrete example of improving AI epistemics: a year ago, no model would use the word "dysbiosis" when given GI map results, but now all four models correctly identify it from just five data points -- demonstrating that persistent user feedback and broadening beyond US-centric medical perspectives (looking at EU, German, and Japanese research) eventually shifts model behavior.
