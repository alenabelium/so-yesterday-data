# AI isn't going how we thought it would

## Executive Summary

David Shapiro explores an unexpected consequence of AI creative tools: the speed at which they let you drain your creative well. Using his experience with Suno (where he produced four albums in weeks then had nothing left for 18 months), he argues that AI removes the natural rate-limiters — instrument practice, band scheduling, approval processes — that previously paced creative output, making the human the sole bottleneck. He identifies a deeper problem: when AI generates exactly what you want, it eliminates the surprise and novelty that make creative consumption satisfying, leading to rapid saturation. The insight extends to productivity burnout at work, where people are using AI chatbots less as the novelty wears off, though new form factors like agents may restart the cycle.

## Key Points

- AI creative tools allow you to exhaust a lifetime of creative inspiration in days — Shapiro produced four music albums on Suno in two weeks, then had nothing to express for 18 months until enough new life experiences accumulated to refill the well. [03:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RsRVI6a0ZdU&t=183)
- The fundamental limitation of AI-generated creative content is that when you steer the output, there is no surprise — even telling an AI to "surprise me" results in regression to the mean, and humans fundamentally need high-entropy external input for creative satisfaction. [11:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RsRVI6a0ZdU&t=719)
- The removal of natural gating functions (approval processes, other people, physical constraints) makes the human the sole bottleneck, which combined with centuries of Calvinist hustle culture, drives the burnout epidemic among heavy AI users. [07:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RsRVI6a0ZdU&t=444)
