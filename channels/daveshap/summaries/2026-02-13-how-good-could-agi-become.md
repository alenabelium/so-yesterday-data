# How GOOD could AGI become?

## Executive Summary

Shapiro explores the best possible outcome for AGI, noting the irony that even Nick Bostrom -- the original AI doomer -- has come full circle to argue we need AGI to survive, echoing what accelerationists have said all along. He challenges the assumption that humans must retain full control, arguing that individuals might actually have 10x or even 100x more agency under benevolent machine governance than under current billionaire-dominated capitalism. The key concept is "metastable attractor states" -- systems of values and incentives that self-correct even without human oversight, analogous to how democracy has spread from 15% to 80% of nations over the last century. He warns that the real near-term risk is billionaires building unregulated industrial bases in space where Earth laws cannot reach them.

## Key Points

- Nick Bostrom's pivot from "AGI will definitely kill us" to "we need AGI to survive" mirrors horseshoe theory -- the doomers and accelerationists have converged on the same conclusion from opposite directions, vindicating the accelerationist position that the only path forward requires building AGI rather than avoiding it. - Moral fading is an underappreciated risk of continuous online learning in AI systems -- just as humans gradually normalize unethical behavior, AI agents with mutable weights could drift from tolerating small harms to larger ones, and this risk was not addressed by leading safety thinkers like Bostrom, Yudkowsky, or Tegmark despite being a fundamental concern for any system that can rewrite its own values. [17:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JNbolQCsJqE&t=1077)
- The optimal future is a "metastable attractor state" where AGI governance self-corrects toward human thriving without requiring a leash -- achieved through correct initial values and path dependency, analogous to how democracy acts as an infectious self-correcting system, with the goal being a solar-punk future where every individual has dramatically more optionality than under current economic structures.
