# Where AI is going in 2026

## Executive Summary

David Shapiro argues that AI tools like Claude Code and Notebook LM have crossed a critical "utility tipping point" where they perform genuine cognitive offload, allowing users to become supervisors rather than doing low-level mental work themselves. He traces how dozens of incremental research advances — from DeepSeek's efficient architectures to recursive language models — get rolled into each new generation of frontier models, producing compounding capability gains. The resulting network effect means these general-purpose tools are now spilling over into domains far beyond coding, including economics research and scientific writing. Shapiro warns that normalcy bias blinds most people to where exponential trends are heading, even as the data clearly shows AI writing 90%+ of accepted code and rapidly subsuming validation tasks too.

## Key Points

- AI tools like Claude Code have reached a "utility tipping point" where they function as general-purpose cognitive offload engines, triggering a network effect as researchers across disciplines — economists, law professors, scientists — adopt them for tasks far beyond coding. [02:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZtQjILC-m3I&t=176)
- Each frontier model (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) has distinct strengths: ChatGPT excels at methodological rigor, Gemini is the most creative but prone to sycophancy and hallucination, and Claude occupies a middle ground but can get stuck in "epistemic humility" loops that derail speculative conversations. [11:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZtQjILC-m3I&t=690)
- Normalcy bias keeps most people from anticipating the next wave — the job of "validating AI output" that emerged six months ago will itself be automated by AI checking AI, and the spillover effects of general-purpose AI tools will reshape research, writing, and knowledge work broadly. [20:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZtQjILC-m3I&t=1231)
