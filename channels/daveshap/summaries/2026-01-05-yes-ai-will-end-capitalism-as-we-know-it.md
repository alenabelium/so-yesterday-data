# Yes, AI will end capitalism (as we know it)

## Executive Summary

David Shapiro argues that AI and robotics will end capitalism as we know it by severing the link between wages and household income that currently drives 60-80% of GDP. Since machines are poised to replace human labor across strength, dexterity, cognition, and empathy, the economy needs new mechanisms for household income beyond wages. He proposes a three-bucket approach: preserving some transfers like UBI as a floor, but primarily broadening capital participation through sovereign wealth funds and employee ownership structures. Shapiro introduces the concept of "generative mutualism" — the evolutionary principle that cooperation and endosymbiosis repeatedly produce superior outcomes — as the new narrative to replace the old social contract between elites and labor, arguing that in an internet era with AI, citizens can coordinate without traditional elite intermediaries.

## Key Points

- The consumption economy requires household spending (60-80% of GDP), not labor per se — so the path forward is broadening capital participation through sovereign wealth funds (like Norway's $1.3T fund or Alaska's Permanent Fund) and employee ownership rather than relying solely on UBI, which loses price signals and becomes a political football. [03:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Llyxebn4dHw&t=187)
- The concept of "double bilateral dependence" — where elites provide protection and prosperity while people provide service and revenue — underpins every historical government, but AI and robotics threaten to break this social contract entirely, raising the question of why elites would keep citizens around and, more importantly, why citizens would keep elites around. [10:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Llyxebn4dHw&t=629)
- In the emerging "attention preference economy," figures like Elon Musk and MrBeast demonstrate that controlling attention, vision, and reputation is the new coordination mechanism — and with internet tools, AI, and decentralized governance, the masses can potentially organize and coordinate resources without traditional elite intermediaries.
