# *Singularity Tingles Intensify*

## Executive Summary

David Shapiro argues that the singularity feels more tangible than ever as 2026 shapes up to be the year AI begins accelerating science itself. He attributes the collective psychological shift to a threshold effect: once people realize AI is smarter than them and improving exponentially, a phase change occurs in how they relate to the technology. Shapiro explains this delayed recognition through "normalcy bias" — our chimpanzee brains evolved for local, geometric environments and lack neural machinery for thinking globally and exponentially. He introduces the concept of "cognitive lacuna," a parallel to cognitive dissonance where one senses a missing shape in understanding, and predicts this will be the next frontier in AI training — models that can identify gaps in their own knowledge and pursue novel insights.

## Key Points

- Normalcy bias is a deeply adaptive cognitive trait from our evolutionary past — our brains constantly reassert that the current moment is normal and permanent — which makes humanity collectively terrible at preparing for exponential AI progress, even when the data clearly shows hockey-stick capability growth. [01:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fVx_u_P9Z5A&t=72)
- A striking poll reveals nearly half of Americans who have used ChatGPT believe it fetches responses from a database rather than generating them in real time, demonstrating how large the gap is between the cutting edge and mainstream understanding of what AI actually is and why it keeps improving. [12:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fVx_u_P9Z5A&t=777)
- Shapiro introduces "cognitive lacuna" — the sensation of orbiting a missing concept you can sense but cannot yet articulate — as the next training frontier for AI models, arguing that once models can identify epistemic gaps and pursue them, they will unlock the kind of scientific intuition that drives genuine discovery.
