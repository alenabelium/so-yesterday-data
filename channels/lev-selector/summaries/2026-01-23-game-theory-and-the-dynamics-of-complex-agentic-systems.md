# Game Theory and the Dynamics of Complex Agentic Systems

## Executive Summary

Guest presenter Maxim, a Concordia CS student, explains why standard reinforcement learning fails for multi-agent systems and how evolutionary game theory (EGT) offers a better framework. The key insight is "survival of the flattest" -- in dynamic environments, the most resilient (not the theoretically optimal) strategy wins, much like how the dodo's perfect adaptation to a static environment made it fragile to change. The talk covers replicator dynamics, the computational limits of modeling self-replicating agents, and the ethical challenges of creating systems that mimic life closely enough to be runaway experiments.

## Key Points

- Standard reinforcement learning breaks down in multi-agent systems because agent outcomes depend on each other's actions in constantly changing environments; game theory provides the meta-framework to model these interdependencies and understand dynamics, not just final equilibrium states. [04:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nm-OnKyBNbk&t=271)
- "Survival of the flattest" means the most resilient strategy outlasts the theoretically optimal one in mutation-rich environments -- like the dodo that was perfectly evolved for a static island but went extinct when conditions changed, overly optimized agents fail when their environment shifts. [09:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nm-OnKyBNbk&t=541)
- Practical barriers to evolutionary agent research include extreme computational demands of running multiple high-cognition models long enough to observe emergent behavior, ethical concerns about self-replicating systems, and fundamental computability limits like the halting problem that constrain what agents can reliably solve. [12:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nm-OnKyBNbk&t=769)
