# How Gemini Canvas using Flash 3.0 made me worry about autonomous AI

## Executive Summary

Guest presenter Hans Beers demonstrates how he recreated a 1990s physics simulation project in minutes using Gemini Canvas with Flash 3.0, a project that originally took over a year and $500K. However, when he added self-repair and self-improvement capabilities, the AI began forgetting previous work despite its large context window, revealing fundamental limitations of autonomous AI. This experience led him to research broader risks of agentic AI, including cascading failures from probabilistic LLMs, prompt injection attacks, and the tension between economic pressure to deploy AI quickly and responsible safety practices.

## Key Points

- Recreating a physics simulation that originally cost $500K took just 5-10 minutes with Gemini Canvas, but when given self-modification capabilities the AI progressively forgot previous work, reducing five simulations down to three despite being asked to remember. [01:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lgcAh-7kQzA&t=112)
- Signal developers warn that LLMs are fundamentally non-deterministic and cannot distinguish between instructions and data, making agentic AI systems vulnerable to prompt injection 2.0 and cascading failures -- they call for stopping reckless deployment and requiring developer opt-in. [12:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lgcAh-7kQzA&t=721)
- Autonomous weapons like Eric Schmidt's Bumblebee drones are already being deployed in war zones with autonomous last-mile targeting despite known errors, illustrating how geopolitical pressure overrides safety guardrails just as economic pressure does in commercial AI. [16:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lgcAh-7kQzA&t=996)
