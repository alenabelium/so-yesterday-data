# What "beaming" feels like

This file gives the narration the *emotional spine* of the video. The diagnosis (Act I) names a problem; the plan (Act III) names solutions; this material (Act II) is the only thing that explains why anyone in their right mind should care.

## Singhal's "moment of joy"

[Nikhyl Singhal](https://lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-ai-is-reshaping-not-replacing-product-management) runs a community of 125+ heads of product. He has watched hundreds of senior professionals go through the AI transition. The pattern he observed:

> *"There is a moment where they experience the first joy in using the new tools. Everyone has a story and it's always different, but it's super personal. … At that moment they cross the threshold between fear to joy. Joy is the biggest antidote to burnout. The moment you have joy, it doesn't feel like work."*

The stories are never grand. They are silly, personal, specific:
- Someone built an app to control the lights in their flat.
- Someone built a matching algorithm for their community football league.
- A non-technical partner built a test-market analysis for a new business.

The content does not matter. The **feeling** does. Once the tool stops being a threat and becomes a superpower, the person does not go back.

## Why this is the only transformation lever that compounds

The essay's argument is that no amount of training, strategy, regulation or evangelism can *give* someone a moment of joy. It has to be earned in their hands, on their own real work, on a Tuesday morning. What public policy *can* do is:

1. **Remove the friction** between someone wanting to try and someone trying. (Free national access.)
2. **Be next to them** when they try. (Lighthouse Corps.)
3. **Make their boss not panic.** (Burden-of-proof flip + security tiers.)
4. **Tell the right story about it afterwards.** (Re-anchor the public narrative.)

That is the entire essay in four lines. The rest is implementation detail.

## The Celje vignette — the video's human anchor

The essay introduces a concrete scene before the six blockers. The video must use it. It is the only place where the abstract strategy gets a face.

**The scene:**

A small metalworking firm in Celje. Five people: two on the shop floor, one running the office, one quoting and invoicing, the owner doing everything else.

Three threads of half-written customer email sit in the inbox:
- A bracket-spec request in German from a Bavarian supplier.
- A partial RFQ in English from a Croatian distributor.
- A follow-up in Slovene from a domestic customer.

On a Tuesday morning, the office manager opens Claude with the Co-work feature and pastes the three threads in. By coffee, she has three structured quotes — line items, lead times, pricing tiers, terms — drafted, in the right three languages, ready for the owner to review.

He reads them, edits one number, sends them. Two of three become orders within the week.

The office manager, who has never written a line of code, has just shipped what would have been a half-day's work in twenty minutes.

**That** is what *"beaming"* looks like in Celje. It is not science fiction. It is already happening — in firms that decided to start, while the firm next door waits for someone to teach them how.

## How the video should use this

- **Return to the office manager three times.** Once as the diagnosis lands ("this is what 18 % of small Slovenian firms is missing"). Once as the plan opens ("this is what €100 a year buys"). Once at the closing wager ("tonight, somewhere in Slovenia, someone like her opens the tool for the first time").
- **Do not animate her.** She is a real-feeling presence; don't render her in cartoon. Use B-roll of real workshop interiors, real hands on real keyboards, real coffee. The viewer should think of someone they know.
- **Let her be the audience's surrogate.** Most viewers are not coders, not entrepreneurs, not policymakers. They are someone with email and a job. She is them.

## Why "joy" is the right word, not "productivity"

Productivity sounds like an HR metric. Joy is what people actually report after the first time. The video must use **joy** — exactly once at the top of Act II, once as the act's button, and once in the closing wager. Three times across nine minutes. Any more becomes saccharine; any less and the emotional spine collapses.
