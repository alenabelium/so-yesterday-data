# Why most people don't cross the threshold

This file gives NotebookLM the *causal* story behind the six blockers in the essay. The narration should make viewers feel the gravity of these patterns — not list them as bullet points.

## 1. The three-week cliff

Microsoft WorkLab tracks Copilot adopters over time. **Around 11 weeks** is the breakthrough moment for those who stay with the tool: most then report gains in productivity, enjoyment, work-life balance, and fewer meetings. The problem: **most people do not stay long enough to reach week 11**. Without workflow-level support — without anyone modelling how to use the tool on real work — the initial novelty fades, the early mistakes feel like proof the tool isn't ready, and people quietly revert to old workflows.

This is *not* a tool failure. It is an adoption failure. It is the gap between "I have access" and "I have a habit." The internal corpus calls this the **three-week cliff**.

**For the video:** the cliff is the reason national access without a Lighthouse Corps would fail. Access alone does not produce a moment of joy. Someone has to be next to the user when the first real task lands.

## 2. The permission gap

In conscientious, hierarchical organisations — schools, ministries, hospitals, large SMEs — the second wall is psychological-procedural: people wait for someone to *tell them* it is okay to use AI for real work. GDPR anxiety, comms-department caution, headteacher risk-aversion, "what about confidentiality" — each individually reasonable, collectively paralytic.

The Shopify memo broke this by **flipping the burden of proof**: instead of "prove AI is safe before using it," the new default is "prove AI cannot do this before asking for more headcount." That single inversion converted a perpetual permission-seeking state into a forward-leaning operating posture.

**For the video:** the burden-of-proof flip is the single highest-leverage policy intervention in Move #2 of the plan. It costs nothing to enact and changes culture inside one quarter.

## 3. The compounding gap

Every week of delay is not one week of missed productivity. It is one week in which:

- The frontier model gets better (and your mental model of "what AI can do" gets one week more outdated).
- Your peer who started six months ago does another 30 days of calibration — learns one more failure mode, encodes one more reusable workflow, builds one more reliable habit.
- The market signal hardens: hiring, salaries, promotions, internal status tilt further toward people who have crossed the threshold.

This is why a **two-year delay is not a 20–30 % productivity gap. It is plausibly 5–10×** in the workflows AI most reshapes. Not because the late adopter is incompetent, but because the early adopter has been quietly recalibrating their entire mental model of what one person can ship in a day.

**For the video:** the compounding gap is the strategic warning under the wager. It is not a measured forecast — frame it as such — but the *direction* is empirically grounded.

## 4. How these three combine to produce stalled countries

The three-week cliff stops individuals.
The permission gap stops institutions.
The compounding gap is what makes the cost of both unrecoverable.

A country trying to "wait and see" is choosing all three at once. That is what Slovenia, the EU more broadly, and most legacy organisations are doing right now. The essay's plan exists to break each of the three in parallel:

- Frontier-seat access for all *removes the friction* at the cliff.
- The Lighthouse Corps *provides the workflow-level support* that gets users past week 11.
- The burden-of-proof flip *closes the permission gap*.
- All three together *reduce the compounding gap* — not eliminate, but materially reduce.

The video should make viewers feel that these are not abstract policy levers. They are surgical interventions on three specific human-and-organisational dynamics that have been measured and named.
