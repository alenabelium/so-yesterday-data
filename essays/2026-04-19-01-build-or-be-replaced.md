# Build or Be Replaced: The PM's New Job Description

> *Watch the [video deep dive](https://youtu.be/I5bvJfeK2ds) for a conversation exploring these ideas.*

> *This essay builds on Nikhyl Singhal's analysis in ["How AI is reshaping (not replacing) product management"](/videos/yUohoaC8_Hs) (Lenny's Podcast, April 19, 2026), synthesized with frameworks from Keith Rabois, Marc Andreessen, and the so-yesterday.ai knowledge base on AI transformation.*

---

Nikhyl Singhal has a number: 30,000 shed, 8,000 rehired.

The former Meta and Google exec, who runs the Skip community for 125+ heads of product, delivered this prediction on Lenny's Podcast with the calm of someone reporting the weather. In the next 12-24 months, companies will fire tens of thousands and hire back a fraction — but the fraction will be "all AI-first." The 30,000 who leave carry skills that AI now handles. The 8,000 who arrive carry skills that AI cannot.

The product management job didn't disappear. It split in two. And the wrong half is dying.

Open PM roles are at a three-year high. Compensation for top builders is at an all-time record. Fourteen of 125 senior PMs in Singhal's community have become founders. For builders — people who love hands-on creation — this is, in his words, "a complete renaissance." For the other half — the information movers, the slide-deck coordinators, the people whose value was routing decisions between meetings — the future is already here, and it is not kind.

Three things matter now. Know which side you're on. Understand why the best performers are the most stuck. And find the one thing that actually gets you through: not fear, not discipline — joy.

## 1. The PM Triage: Know Where You Sit

Every PM today sits in one of four quadrants defined by two questions: **do you build?** and **do you judge?**

```
                      BUILDS (hands-on creation)
                      High               Low
                 ┌──────────────┬──────────────┐
        High     │   ARCHITECT  │  STRATEGIST  │
   JUDGES        │   thriving   │   at risk    │
   (evaluates    ├──────────────┼──────────────┤
    quality)     │    BUILDER   │   SHUFFLER   │
        Low      │   emerging   │   obsolete   │
                 └──────────────┴──────────────┘
```

**The Architect** builds and judges. They prototype by Tuesday, evaluate 100 AI-generated iterations by Friday, and ship the one that matters on Monday. Keith Rabois, speaking on Lenny's the week before Singhal, framed it bluntly: ["The skill is now more like being a CEO."](/videos/xCd9ykretlg) The Architect doesn't manage roadmaps — they notice what just became possible this week and create value next week.

**The Strategist** has judgment but delegates all execution. This was the ideal PM five years ago. Today, judgment without building context becomes shallow. When AI generates 100 variations in an afternoon, the PM who has never built anything cannot evaluate them meaningfully.

**The Builder** ships rapidly but lacks evaluation depth. The junior PM or "[vibe coder](/knowledge/vibe-coding)" who produces fast with AI but doesn't yet know what "good" looks like at scale. Lazar Yavanovich, the first professional vibe coder at Lovable, spends [80% of his time in planning and chat mode and only 20% executing](/videos/0XNkUdzxiZI). The path is clear: build judgment through repetition.

**The Shuffler** neither builds nor judges. They coordinate, route information, maintain the Jira board. Singhal's term is "information mover," and his prognosis is direct: roughly half of existing PMs fall here.

Marc Andreessen described the convergence behind this split as a ["Mexican standoff"](/videos/87Pm0SGTtN8): PMs, engineers, and designers each now believe they can do the other two roles with AI — and they're all kind of right. The three roles are collapsing into one atomic function: **the builder**. Zevy Arnowitz, a PM at Meta with zero technical background, [ships production-quality products](/videos/1em64iUFt3U) using only AI tools and slash commands. The [ACM now recognizes vibe coding as a PM competency](https://cacm.acm.org/blogcacm/the-vibe-coding-imperative-for-product-managers/). The PM who waits two sprints for engineering bandwidth is being replaced by the PM who built it over a weekend.

**A self-assessment.** Singhal suggests a brutally simple exercise: put green, yellow, and red next to every meeting you had this week, then look at the color chart. "The vast majority of PMs show mostly yellow and red. The ones moving into build mode — it's mostly green and yellow." The color of your calendar is the color of your future.

## The Evidence: Teams, Shedding, and What's Coming

Before we get to why people don't change, the data on what's changing:

**Teams are shrinking.** At PayPal, Rabois found only 12-17 "barrels" (people who independently drive initiatives) among 254 employees. In the AI era, much of the "ammunition" work transfers to agents. Nate Jones identifies the optimal unit as [~5 people](/videos/zhXgkQ3nYeE) pointed at missions 5-10x larger than before. At Kimi, a PM launches 3 agents before lunch and has 70% of implementation by 11:30 AM. Companies like Cursor achieve [$5M revenue per employee](/knowledge/ai-native-economics). Singhal adds: CPOs are building internal AI tools to automate their own product operating systems — automated reviews, standups, status reports — because when 10-100x more changes flow through the pipeline, manual judgment doesn't scale.

**The shedding is real.** Q1 2026: [78,557 tech layoffs, 48% AI-attributed](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/tech-industry-lays-off-nearly-80-000-employees-in-the-first-quarter-of-2026). Mid-level coordination roles are the most vulnerable. McKinsey cut 5,000 of its own people while deploying 12,000 AI agents. Shopify's "Red Queen" memo requires teams to [prove AI can't do the work](/videos/3BhM3m4K0bY) before requesting headcount.

**The rehiring is different.** Brand logos on resumes matter less than demonstrated modernity. Interviews test tool usage and judgment through scenarios, not past shipping experience. The [entry-level pipeline has collapsed](/essays/2026-03-13-03-72-days-that-rewrote-the-rules): hiring down 67%, new graduates at 7% of hires. "AI-first PM" is not "PM who uses ChatGPT." It's a fundamentally different role.

## 2. The Shadow Superpower: Why the Best Are the Most Stuck

Here is the most counterintuitive insight in Singhal's analysis — and the most important for anyone who considers themselves a high performer:

> *"The ones that were the best at working in the past — the ones that mastered the old game — find it the hardest to go through this reinvention stage. The better you are at mastering one system, the less likely you are to recognize the new one. If you're really good, you have no incentive to change."*

He calls it the shadow superpower. Your greatest strength — the deep expertise, the finely tuned instincts, the muscle memory of how to navigate organizations — becomes the thing that prevents you from seeing the new game. The METR study confirmed this with data: experienced developers using AI tools were [19% slower](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) than without them — while believing they were 24% faster. The perception-reality gap is not a bug. It's your expertise protecting its own relevance.

This is why [identity threat](/knowledge/identity-threat) hits senior PMs hardest. "I spent 10 years becoming great at this. Now you're telling me a junior with Claude is faster?" Shreyas Doshi observed the two poles: some PMs avoid AI entirely (protecting their identity), others over-rely on it for everything (surrendering it). Both are identity responses. Neither works.

Three walls block the transition:

**Wall 1: The disappointment algorithm.** Singhal names the reality that no self-help book will: "Your goal in your power years is to equally disappoint everyone in your life. You have 6 hours to give and 20 hours of demand. Your parents will not be more disappointed than your kids, than your health, than your partner, than your work. Now you're telling me the number one thing is to reinvent when I'm barely able to manage this disappointment algorithm." Reinvention requires time. Time is the one thing a mid-career PM does not have.

**Wall 2: The [permission gap](/knowledge/permission-gap).** 80% of trained AI users abandon tools within [three weeks](/knowledge/three-week-cliff). Not because the tools failed — because of effort-value confusion. An 8-hour manually researched report *feels* productive. A 10-minute AI version feels like cheating. People chase validation signals from the old world — brand logos, manual rigor, prestigious titles — while the new world rewards speed, judgment, and output.

**Wall 3: The moving target.** Even if you take a week off to learn the tools, three months later that knowledge is antiquated. "It's not like I went from not having a job to having a new job and then I'm back in flow. I'm *never* in flow because the target keeps changing." The exhaustion of perpetual reinvention on top of the disappointment algorithm on top of the shadow superpower creates a paralyzing stack.

But here's the relief Singhal offers: **it won't be forever.** "After a couple of years, things will settle. Companies will be built a certain way. The job you had next will look like the previous job." The chaos is real but finite. The question is whether you cross the threshold before it settles — or after, when the positions are filled.

## 3. The Moment of Joy: How You Actually Cross

The conventional advice is discipline: set aside 30 minutes a day, push through the discomfort, build habits. It's correct but insufficient. Singhal has watched hundreds of PMs go through this transition, and the pattern he sees is different. It's not discipline that gets people through the tunnel. It's joy.

> *"There is a moment where they experience the first joy in using the new tools. Everyone has a story and it's always different, but it's super personal. 'My partner and I use this app that I built.' 'I built a chief of staff app to keep track of my inbox.' Even my wife has a story... And at that moment they cross the threshold between fear to joy."*

The stories are never grand. They're silly, personal, specific. Someone stayed up all night building an app to control their house lights. Someone built a matching algorithm for their community. Someone's non-technical spouse built a test market analysis for a new business idea. The content doesn't matter. The *feeling* matters — the moment where the tool stops being a threat and becomes a superpower.

> *"Joy is the biggest antidote to burnout. The moment you have joy, it doesn't feel like work. Most product management feels like work if you're not building. We are now moving into a world where product management will be building and joy, not work."*

This is the key reframe. The essay is called "Build or Be Replaced," and that sounds like a threat. But the real message is an invitation: **the things you don't love about your PM job — the routing, the status updates, the theatrics, the information moving — those are the things AI is taking away.** What remains is building, judging, creating. The green parts of your calendar.

And once you find that first moment of joy — once you build one silly thing and feel the spark — something shifts. The [compounding gap](/knowledge/compounding-gap) starts working in your favor. You find time you didn't know you had, because the work stops feeling like a sacrifice and starts feeling like play. The disappointment algorithm recalculates, because joyful work creates energy instead of consuming it.

**The practical path:**

**Find your moment of joy.** Not a work project — something personal. An app for your family. A tool that fixes your inbox. Something where the only stakeholder is you, and the only definition of success is "does this spark something?" Singhal's advice: "If you're a leader, find those moments of joy in your staff, because it's contagious."

**Survive the cliff.** The [three-week cliff](/knowledge/three-week-cliff) is real. 80% quit at this point. Push through with a daily 30-minute build habit (non-negotiable, like exercise), a builder buddy for accountability, and one rule: build first, then do your regular work. Not the reverse.

**Develop judgment through repetition.** Build 10 things. Throw away 8. The 2 you keep teach you what "good" feels like. This is [the 70% problem](/knowledge/the-70-percent-problem) — AI gets to 70% quality instantly, but closing to 95% requires human judgment that only develops through building.

**Swallow your ego.** Singhal is blunt: "I don't want a single person saying 'I was a XYZ leader, I would only consider roles at that same level.' It doesn't matter what we've done in the past. Be willing to take something smaller to go through the tunnel correctly." Stay long-term focused. The cream rises — but only if you're in the right game.

And remember: an [engineer's definition of greatness](/videos/yUohoaC8_Hs) is "someone who obsoletes themselves from everything they do." That was true before AI. AI just put an exclamation point on it.

## The New Job Description

Here's what the PM role looks like in 2027, written as a posting from the future:

> **Product Architect** — Builds prototypes in hours, not sprints. Evaluates 10-100x more iterations than ships. Writes specifications that agent teams execute autonomously. Measures judgment quality and customer impact, not shipping velocity. Has built at least 3 products with AI tools. Can demonstrate taste by explaining what they killed and why.

This job doesn't exist at most companies yet. It will within 18 months. The PMs operating this way — the Architects — are getting record offers, becoming founders, and expanding into roles that didn't exist a year ago.

Singhal's final observation is the one that stays: "What I see in my community is smiling exhaustion. Before it was just exhaustion. I take smiling exhaustion over exhaustion. But the pace is relentless."

Smiling exhaustion. That's the honest description. The pace is brutal and the tunnel is dark and the disappointment algorithm never sleeps. But the people who have crossed — the ones who found their moment of joy, who started building, who let go of the shadow superpower — they're smiling.

The best time to start building was three months ago. The second best time is this afternoon.
