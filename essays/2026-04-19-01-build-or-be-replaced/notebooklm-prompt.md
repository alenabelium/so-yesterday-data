# NotebookLM Prompt — Build or Be Replaced

## For Audio Overview / Video Overview

Upload all numbered source files (01-06) to NotebookLM, then use this prompt:

---

Create an engaging overview about how the product manager role is splitting in two — builders who are thriving and "information movers" who face obsolescence.

Structure around THREE main points:

**Point 1: The PM Triage** — Product management just split into four quadrants: Architects (build + judge = thriving), Strategists (judge but don't build = at risk), Builders (build but lack judgment = emerging), and Shufflers (coordinate only = obsolete). About half of existing PMs are Shufflers and don't know it. Use the green-yellow-red calendar exercise: color-code your meetings for a week — mostly yellow and red means you're on the wrong side.

**Point 2: The Shadow Superpower** — Here's the twist: the BEST performers are the most stuck. Singhal calls it the "shadow superpower" — the better you mastered the old game, the less likely you are to see the new one. Make this vivid with the METR study (experienced developers 19% SLOWER with AI tools while believing they were 24% faster). Then layer on the real barriers: the "disappointment algorithm" (you have 6 hours to give and 20 hours of demand from kids, parents, health, work — and now you're supposed to reinvent too?), the permission gap (80% abandon AI tools in 3 weeks — not tech failure, it's that an 8-hour manual report FEELS more productive than a 10-minute AI one), and the moving target (what you learn this month is outdated in three).

**Point 3: The Moment of Joy** — This is the hopeful turn. What actually gets people through is not discipline or fear — it's joy. Everyone who successfully crossed has a personal story: "I built an app for my partner," "I automated my inbox," "I stayed up all night hacking on something silly." The moment joy hits, everything changes — you find time you didn't have, burnout recedes, the disappointment algorithm recalculates. Joy is the antidote to burnout. End with Singhal's observation: "What I see in my community is smiling exhaustion. Before it was just exhaustion. I take smiling exhaustion over exhaustion."

**Tone:** Empathetic but urgent. Like a smart friend who's been through it and cares enough to be direct. NOT doom-and-gloom. Use personal stories and specific quotes — the vibe coding during TV shows, the wife's business, the engineer who said "greatness is obsoleting yourself." Make it feel human.

**Close with:** "It won't be forever. Things will settle in a couple of years. But right now is the moment to cross the threshold. The best time to start building was three months ago. The second best time is this afternoon."

**Target audience:** Product managers, product leaders, tech workers, and anyone wondering if their role is safe in the AI era.
