Create an engaging video overview about why hesitating on AI adoption is riskier than leaning in -- using the bicycle metaphor where going slower makes balancing harder, not safer.

Focus on THREE main points:

1. **Three psychological walls block adoption, not technical barriers.** The Identity Threat: if you've spent 15 years becoming an expert, being told to "think differently" feels like erasure of everything you've built. Research shows even experienced developers become 19% slower with AI while believing they're 24% faster -- expertise creates dangerous overconfidence. The Permission Gap: 80% of AI users abandoned tools within three weeks because nobody taught the judgment layer -- when to trust, when to push back, how to decompose tasks. Your best employees quit first because they ask "are we allowed to use this?" and, hearing silence, opt out. The Comfort of Familiar Struggle: an 8-hour report feels productive; a 10-minute AI draft feels like cheating -- even when the output is equivalent. We're addicted to effort as a proxy for value.

2. **Six judgment skills matter, not technical ones.** Forget prompt engineering -- it's already obsolete as a standalone skill. What separates thriving professionals: context assembly (curating what information to give AI), quality judgment (knowing when to trust vs. verify), task decomposition (breaking work into AI-appropriate pieces), iterative refinement (moving from 70% to 95%), frontier recognition (knowing what AI handles well today vs. last quarter), and rejection competency (the ability to say "no, this is wrong, and here's specifically why" -- the most undervalued skill of all).

3. **The compounding gap makes delay exponentially costly.** Someone who started six months ago doesn't just have a head start -- they have six months of calibration, encoded constraints, and muscle memory. Because AI capabilities accelerate, the distance between calibrated and uncalibrated widens with every model release. Entry-level hiring is down 67%. The workforce is bifurcating into people who specify and orchestrate vs. people being slowly commoditized. Every quarter of delay creates exponentially more distance, not linear.

Tone: Empathetic but urgent. Acknowledge the real psychological difficulty of this transition -- the identity disorientation is legitimate, the grief over changing how you work is real. But don't let empathy become an excuse for inaction. The bicycle metaphor should run throughout: momentum creates stability, hesitation creates wobble.

Target audience: Mid-career knowledge workers (5-20 years experience) who intellectually understand AI is important but haven't made the transition. They're not resistant -- they're stuck behind psychological walls they may not have named yet.

Target length: 8-10 minutes.
