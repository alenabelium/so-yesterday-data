# Beam or Be Left Behind: A Slovenian Plan to Skip Digital Transformation and Jump Straight Into AI

*Notes from the edge of an AmCham Slovenia panel on AI, work and the next generation of jobs.*

---

There are two numbers worth holding side by side.

The first: in 2025, **20.0% of EU enterprises with 10 or more employees** used AI. Denmark sat at 42%, Finland at 38%, Sweden at 35%. Slovenia, as the moderator of this panel rightly observed, sits below the European average — and the Eurostat data adds a Slovene twist worth naming. The **gap between large and small Slovenian companies is the widest in the EU — 53.5 percentage points** ([Eurostat](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Use_of_artificial_intelligence_in_enterprises)). A handful of large firms are using AI, narrowly, mostly for ICT security. The 99% of the Slovene economy that is SMEs is almost untouched.

The second: the World Economic Forum projects that **roughly 60% of workers will need reskilling or upskilling by 2030**, with 11 of every 100 unlikely to receive it ([Future of Jobs Report 2025](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/)). The OECD puts **28% of occupations** at high exposure to automation ([OECD](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-employment-outlook-2023_08785bba-en.html)).

These two numbers tell one story, not two. *Capability is now cheap. Calibration is scarce.* Frontier intelligence — Opus, Codex, Gemini, Mythos — costs a teacher in Murska Sobota the same as it costs a teacher in Helsinki. What separates them is who has actually sat down with the tool, made it do a real job, felt the floor drop, and never gone back. That experience cannot be transferred by a slide deck. It has to be lived.

This essay argues that Slovenia should stop trying to "build digital competence first, then layer AI on top." That sequence is from a previous era and we no longer have the time. The plan that follows is unfashionably blunt: **give everyone access to frontier agentic systems by September 2026, flip the burden of proof, and recruit a few hundred evangelists to make sure people actually cross the threshold.**

## The thing the data won't show you: "beaming"

Anyone who has built something real with an agentic system in the last six months will recognise this feeling. You write three paragraphs of intent. You walk away to make coffee. You come back to a working prototype, a draft report, a refactored codebase, a meeting summary with action items, a literature review with cited sources — done. Not perfect. But done in twelve minutes what would have taken twelve hours, by something that asked you good clarifying questions along the way.

The first time it happens, you laugh. The second time, you change what you commit to do next week. The third time, you cannot remember how you used to work.

Nikhyl Singhal, who runs a community of 125+ heads of product, [calls this the "moment of joy"](/essays/2026-04-19-01-build-or-be-replaced) and says it is the only thing that reliably gets people across the transformation tunnel. Not discipline. Not training. Joy. *"The moment you have joy, it doesn't feel like work."*

Until that moment hits, AI sounds like marketing. After it hits, the old workflow looks like a tape recorder. The Slovene problem is not that we lack a strategy. It is that almost no one in the country has been beamed yet — and you cannot legislate, fund or train your way into someone else's first joy moment. You have to put the tool in their hands, remove the obstacles, and let them stumble into it.

## Why "build digital competence first" is the wrong sequence

The reflex in Slovenian institutional thinking — and in most of Europe — is to sequence the transformation: first run a digital literacy programme, then train teachers, then maybe pilot AI in a few schools, then write a strategy, then procure, then deploy. Each step takes 12–24 months. By the time you reach the fifth box, the tools you procured against are two generations obsolete.

This is the inversion that the AI frontier forces. Andrej Karpathy's old line — *you don't need to be educated to use AI, you should just get frontier AI and educate yourself* — is now operational reality. The cheapest, fastest, most personalised tutor a Slovene fifth-grader will ever encounter is already running on a server in Frankfurt. The cheapest, most patient mentor an over-55 civil servant in Velenje will ever encounter is the same model. Knowledge has become almost free. **Access and the willingness to use it are the only remaining scarcities.**

This is the lesson from Estonia. While Slovenia debates curriculum reform, Estonia's [AI Leap programme](https://www.educationestonia.org/estonia-launches-ai-leap-2025-to-transform-education/) put frontier AI tools — through public partnerships with OpenAI and Anthropic — into the hands of **20,000 high-school students and 3,000 teachers from September 2025**, with the explicit plan to extend further. Note the move: Estonia did not write a curriculum, then train teachers, then deploy tools. It deployed tools, then let the curriculum self-organise around what worked. That is the Estonian instinct that built the digital state in ten years and the one Slovenia should borrow now.

Finland's instinct is the other half. Its [phenomenon-based learning](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/2331186X.2024.2309733) reform pushed schools to organise teaching around real-world phenomena — climate, migration, energy — across subject boundaries. The pedagogical match with agentic AI is almost unfair: an agent that can pull together history, chemistry, statistics and policy in one conversation is the perfect phenomenon-based tutor. Slovenia should not pick Estonia *or* Finland. It should run Estonia's deployment speed on Finland's pedagogy.

## Six blockers, in plain Slovene

Before any plan, name what actually stops people. Each of these is a wall a Slovene teacher, civil servant or SME owner is hitting *this month*.

1. **Access friction.** Frontier tools cost €17–€200 per month per seat. To an Anthropic engineer that is a coffee. To a primary-school teacher in Pomurje it is a real budget decision that requires a school director's sign-off and a procurement procedure. Most never start.
2. **"It's not there yet" fatigue.** Many people tried ChatGPT in 2023, were unimpressed, and have not tried frontier agentic systems since. Their mental model of AI is 2½ years old, in a field where six months is a generation. The [compounding gap](/knowledge/compounding-gap) keeps widening while they wait.
3. **The wrong learning posture.** People still want to be *taught* AI — a course, a certificate, a workshop. The actual learning loop is: take one real task today, do it with the AI, push back when it is wrong, ship the result. Knowledge accrual mindset is dead; **edge living** is the replacement.
4. **Institutional fear.** Public-sector procurement, GDPR anxiety, headteacher caution, hospital ethics committees, ministry comms departments — each is individually reasonable, collectively paralytic. Most Slovene institutions are afraid both of starting and of having "inexperienced" people leading the start. So no one starts.
5. **No evangelists.** Estonians have public figures who light sparks. Finland has whole school networks. Slovenia has a handful of LinkedIn voices and a lot of quiet expertise. The country lacks a visible, recognised cadre of "lighthouses" — people who can sit down with a sceptical SME owner for an hour and produce a *moment of joy*.
6. **The wrong story.** Public discourse oscillates between "AI will take your job" and "AI is overhyped." Both stories deflate agency. The accurate story is harder and more energising: *AI is a one-in-a-generation cognitive expansion. You will work with it. The window to learn it well is short. Tomorrow is too late.*

These six blockers map directly to what we know empirically. Microsoft found that **80% of trained AI users abandon the tools within three weeks** — the [three-week cliff](/knowledge/three-week-cliff) — and not because the tools failed, but because nobody taught them the middle layer: when to trust, when to push back, how to decompose. Shopify's "Red Queen" memo broke through this by **flipping the burden of proof**: teams had to prove AI *couldn't* do something before requesting more headcount. Productivity rose 30% inside a year ([Stop Overthinking It](/essays/2026-03-13-01-start-with-ai-today)).

## The plan: six moves Slovenia can make in months, not years

This is what a serious Slovene response looks like. Each move is concrete, fundable, and can begin before the next budget cycle.

### 1. Frontier-tool access for all, by September 2026

Treat agentic AI like the country treated Zoom and MS Teams during COVID. Procure a national framework giving **every primary, secondary and tertiary student, every teacher, every civil servant, and every employee of a registered SME** seat access to at least one frontier agentic system (Claude, Codex, Gemini at minimum) at the highest tier those vendors will negotiate. Co-finance with EU Digital Decade funds and the existing NpUI envelope. Cost ceiling: well under €100 per seat per year at national scale; the EU AI Continent strategy explicitly funds exactly this kind of move ([European Parliament briefing](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2025/775923/EPRS_BRI(2025)775923_EN.pdf)). The model is Estonia's AI Leap — but extended from 20,000 students to the entire active population.

### 2. Flip the burden of proof in the public sector

A simple administrative instruction from the Prime Minister's office, modelled on the [Shopify memo](/videos/dzp0OQbElpU): **before requesting a new full-time position or external consulting engagement, every public body must document why an agentic system cannot do the work.** Not a ban on hiring. A default that forces the question. Pair with a small "agentic exemptions" team in the Ministry of Public Administration that can rule on edge cases inside two weeks. The Shopify rule changed company culture inside one quarter. There is no structural reason it could not change ministerial culture inside two.

### 3. A Slovenian "Lighthouse Corps"

Recruit 200–300 evangelists across the country — teachers, doctors, engineers, lawyers, accountants, mayors, public-sector middle managers, museum curators — and give them three things: top-tier frontier access, a small honorarium, and an explicit mandate to *light sparks*. Their job is not to train. Their job is to sit down with sceptics one afternoon a week and produce a moment of joy. Track one metric only: "first joy moments" per quarter. The Lighthouse Corps is the human network that turns access into experience and experience into contagion. It is the missing component every Slovene transformation plan has ever lacked.

### 4. Solve confidentiality and security at the platform layer, not the user layer

The single fastest way to keep institutions from blocking adoption is to remove the security objection from individual users. Sign EU-region data processing agreements with frontier vendors at the national level. Stand up a small national "agentic safety" team — three people, not thirty — to publish a simple, living playbook: what data classes go into which tier of tool, what gets local, what gets routed through air-gapped instances, what is just fine to put into Claude tomorrow morning. Make the default *permissive with a clear rule for exceptions*, not *restrictive with vague guidance*. The McKinsey-style breach pattern ([Daily Digest, May 11](/digests/2026-05-11)) makes this urgent — but it is a platform problem to solve once, not a barrier each teacher should re-litigate.

### 5. Bring the SME 99% along — through chambers, not consultants

Slovenia's 53.5-point gap between large and small firms is the single biggest leverage point in the economy. The chambers — GZS, OZS, and the AmCham community itself — are the right delivery vehicles. Three concrete instruments: (a) free frontier seat access for the first year for any registered SME that completes a 2-hour "first beam" workshop; (b) a *vavčer* programme structured around outcomes (one shipped agentic workflow), not hours of consulting; (c) sector-specific "agentic playbooks" co-authored by Lighthouse Corps practitioners — a turism playbook, a metals-and-machinery playbook, a logistics playbook — published openly, in Slovene. The Klarna lesson — one AI assistant doing the work of 700 human agents ([The Intelligence Economy](/essays/2026-03-21-01-the-intelligence-economy)) — is not just for unicorns. The mid-sized Slovene manufacturer who runs a five-person ops team can capture proportionally similar gains, but only if someone shows them how.

### 6. Re-anchor the public story: this is a cognitive expansion, not a job-loss event

The Finnish education reform succeeded partly because it was sold as *liberating children's agency*, not as catching up to PISA scores. Slovenia needs the same reframing for AI. The right line — for ministers, school directors, parents, union leaders, journalists — is something like: **"This is a once-in-a-generation chance to expand what every Slovene can know and do. We can either work with it, starting now, or watch other small countries pull away. We are choosing now."** The doom story disempowers; the cognitive-expansion story conscripts. Choose the one that creates agents, not victims.

## Answers to the panel, compressed

The moderator's questions deserve direct answers. Here they are, in the spirit of a 3–5 minute slot:

**Which number speaks more to you — Slovenia below the EU average, or 60% needing reskilling by 2030?** The reskilling number, because the first is fixable in 18 months while the second describes a wave that is already breaking. Q1 2026 alone saw 78,557 tech layoffs globally with 48% AI-attributed ([72 Days That Rewrote the Rules](/essays/2026-03-13-03-72-days-that-rewrote-the-rules)). The clock is the story.

**What do graduates bring that AI cannot?** Taste, judgement, and the willingness to push back. Code, drafts, analyses — those are now infrastructure. We are not hiring for execution; we are hiring for [rejection competency](/knowledge/rejection-competency) and [specification quality](/knowledge/specification-quality). If a graduate cannot tell us *why* an AI output is wrong in their domain, the graduate is not yet useful. If they can, they are worth three of the old kind.

**Skills shortage as the main obstacle — have you felt it, who adapted?** Always the same surprise: it is not the most technical or the most senior. It is the people with high agency and low ego — the ones willing to sit with the tool, get bad results, push back, and try again. Often mid-career. Often women returning after a break. Often not the ones we expected.

**Where do you trust AI without reservation?** Anywhere the [three pillars](/essays/2026-04-16-01-dark-code-and-the-three-pillars) are strong — specification, tests, code — and where the blast radius is contained. Drafting, summarising, first-pass research, schema migration with backups, refactoring with a green test suite. Where do humans still decide? Hiring people, firing people, allocating capital above a threshold, anything irreversible, anything involving the dignity of a citizen.

**Which professions will emerge?** Specification architects. Verification strategists. Lighthouse-style domain translators. Agent orchestrators. "[Solo operators](/knowledge/solo-operator)" running former-team-sized scope. We do not need to predict perfectly — we need to make sure the *training environments* exist (real frontier tools, real problems) so the new roles can form in Slovenia rather than being imported from Berlin or Tallinn.

**Estonia or Finland?** Both, in this order: Estonia's deployment speed, Finland's pedagogy. Concrete measure for tomorrow: a Government decision putting frontier agentic seats into the hands of every teacher and student by 1 September 2026, and a Lighthouse Corps recruited by 1 July.

**What awaits the entrepreneur who waits two more years?** Their better-prepared peers will have a 5–10× productivity gap, not a 20–30% one. The [compounding gap](/knowledge/compounding-gap) does not wait for strategy documents. The advice to the hesitant in the room is the simplest possible: open Claude or Codex tonight, take one real task from tomorrow's calendar, and do it with the tool. Not a toy task. A real one. **Joy is on the other side, and so is your next decade.**

## The wager

The honest version of Slovenia's AI position is this. We have a small, educated, multilingual population. We have a credible existing AI strategy (NpUI 2020–2025, €110 million) that took us partway. We have one of the EU's widest internal AI adoption gaps and one of its smaller windows to close it. We do not have time for another five-year cycle. We do have time — barely — for a 24-month sprint that puts frontier tools in everyone's hands, flips the public-sector burden of proof, builds a Lighthouse network, and re-anchors the story around cognitive expansion.

The bicycle does not wait for us to feel ready. Estonia did not wait. Finland did not wait. The question is whether Slovenia rides the next 24 months, or gets reshaped by them.

The best time to start was last September. The second best time is the morning after this panel.
