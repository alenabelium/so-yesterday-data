# Beam or Be Left Behind: A Slovenian Plan to Skip Digital Transformation and Jump Straight Into AI

*A practical 24-month strategy for a country that has run out of time for another five-year roadmap.*

---

There are two numbers worth holding side by side.

The first: in 2025, **20.0% of EU enterprises with 10 or more employees** used AI ([Eurostat](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Use_of_artificial_intelligence_in_enterprises)). Denmark sat at 42%, Finland at 38%, Sweden at 35%. Slovenia, at roughly **22%**, is not below the EU headline number — but the headline is the wrong place to look. The Slovene story is **an internal split**: among firms with 10 or more employees, **72% of large enterprises use AI, but only 18% of small ones do** ([SURS](https://www.stat.si/StatWeb/en/News/Index/9885)). That **~54-point gap is the widest in the EU**. AI has reached the large-company layer of the Slovene economy; the small-firm layer — which is where most Slovenes actually work — has not crossed the threshold yet.

The second: the World Economic Forum projects that **roughly 60% of workers will need reskilling or upskilling by 2030**, with 11 of every 100 unlikely to receive it ([Future of Jobs Report 2025](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/)). The OECD puts **28% of occupations** at high exposure to automation ([OECD](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-employment-outlook-2023_08785bba-en.html)).

These two numbers tell one story, not two. *Capability is now cheap. Calibration is scarce.* Frontier agentic systems cost a teacher in Murska Sobota the same as they cost a teacher in Helsinki. What separates them is who has actually sat down with the tool, made it do a real job, felt the floor drop, and never gone back. That experience cannot be transferred by a slide deck. It has to be lived.

This essay argues that Slovenia should stop trying to "build digital competence first, then layer AI on top." That sequence is from a previous era and we no longer have the time. The plan that follows is unfashionably blunt: **give everyone access to frontier agentic systems by September 2026, flip the burden of proof, and recruit a few hundred evangelists who can make sure people actually cross the threshold.**

## The thing the data won't show you: "beaming"

Anyone who has built something real with an agentic system in the last six months will recognise this feeling. You write three paragraphs of intent. You walk away to make coffee. You come back to a working prototype, a draft report, a refactored codebase, a meeting summary with action items, a literature review with cited sources — done. Not perfect. But done in twelve minutes what would have taken twelve hours, by something that asked you good clarifying questions along the way.

The first time it happens, you laugh. The second time, you change what you commit to do next week. The third time, you cannot remember how you used to work.

Nikhyl Singhal, who runs a community of 125+ heads of product, [calls this the "moment of joy"](/essays/2026-04-19-01-build-or-be-replaced) and says it is the only thing that reliably gets people across the transformation tunnel. Not discipline. Not training. Joy. *"The moment you have joy, it doesn't feel like work."*

Until that moment hits, AI sounds like marketing. After it hits, the old workflow looks like a tape recorder. The Slovene problem is not that we lack a strategy. It is that almost no one in the country has been beamed yet — and you cannot legislate, fund or train your way into someone else's first joy moment. You have to put the tool in their hands, remove the obstacles, and let them stumble into it.

## Why "build digital competence first" is the wrong sequence

The reflex in Slovenian institutional thinking — and in most of Europe — is to sequence the transformation: first run a digital literacy programme, then train teachers, then maybe pilot AI in a few schools, then write a strategy, then procure, then deploy. Each step takes 12–24 months. By the time you reach the fifth box, the tools you procured against are two generations obsolete.

This is the inversion that the AI frontier forces. The new operational reality is blunt: *you don't need to be educated to use frontier AI — you should get frontier AI and educate yourself with it.* The cheapest, fastest, most personalised tutor a Slovene fifth-grader will ever encounter is already running on a server in Frankfurt. The cheapest, most patient mentor an over-55 civil servant in Velenje will ever encounter is the same model. Knowledge has become almost free. **Access and the willingness to use it are the only remaining scarcities.**

This is the lesson from Estonia. While Slovenia debates curriculum reform, Estonia's [AI Leap programme](https://www.educationestonia.org/estonia-launches-ai-leap-2025-to-transform-education/) — through public partnerships with OpenAI and Anthropic — put frontier AI tools into the hands of **20,000 high-school students and 3,000 teachers from September 2025**, with explicit plans to extend further. Note the move: Estonia did not wait for a perfect curriculum before putting tools in schools. It paired access with teacher training and adapted from there. That is the Estonian instinct that built the digital state in ten years and the one Slovenia should borrow now.

Finland's instinct is the other half. Its [phenomenon-based learning](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/2331186X.2024.2309733) reform added cross-disciplinary periods organised around real-world phenomena — climate, migration, energy — alongside, not in place of, traditional subjects. The pedagogical match with agentic AI is almost unfair: an agent that can pull together history, chemistry, statistics and policy in one conversation is the perfect phenomenon-based tutor. Slovenia should not pick Estonia *or* Finland. It should run **Estonia's deployment speed on Finland's pedagogy**.

## What this looks like in Celje on a Tuesday morning

Picture a small metalworking firm in Celje. Five people: two on the shop floor, one running the office, one quoting and invoicing, the owner doing everything else. Three threads of half-written customer email sit in the inbox — a bracket-spec request in German from a Bavarian supplier, a partial RFQ in English from a Croatian distributor, a follow-up in Slovene from a domestic customer. On a Tuesday morning, the office manager opens Claude (Code and Co-work) and pastes the three threads in. By coffee, she has three structured quotes — line items, lead times, pricing tiers, terms — drafted, in the right three languages, ready for the owner to review. He reads them, edits one number, sends them. Two of three become orders within the week. The office manager, who has never written a line of code, has just shipped what would have been a half-day's work in twenty minutes. That is what *"beaming"* looks like in Celje. It is not science fiction. It is already happening in firms that decided to start, while the firm next door waits for someone to teach them how.

## Six blockers, named plainly

Before any plan, name what actually stops people. Each of these is a wall a Slovene teacher, civil servant or SME owner is hitting *this month*.

1. **Access friction.** Frontier tools cost €17–€200 per month per seat. To an Anthropic engineer that is a coffee. To a primary-school teacher in Pomurje it is a real budget decision that requires a school director's sign-off and a procurement procedure. Most never start.
2. **"It's not there yet" fatigue.** Many people tried ChatGPT in 2023, were unimpressed, and have not tried frontier agentic systems since. Their mental model of AI is two and a half years old, in a field where six months is a generation. The [compounding gap](/knowledge/compounding-gap) keeps widening while they wait.
3. **The wrong learning posture.** People still want to be *taught* AI — a course, a certificate, a workshop. The actual learning loop is: take one real task today, do it with the AI, push back when it is wrong, ship the result. Knowledge-accrual mindset is dead; **edge living** is the replacement.
4. **Institutional fear.** Public-sector procurement, GDPR anxiety, headteacher caution, hospital ethics committees, ministry comms departments — each individually reasonable, collectively paralytic. Most Slovene institutions are afraid both of starting and of having "inexperienced" people leading the start. So no one starts.
5. **No evangelists.** Estonia has public figures who light sparks. Finland has whole school networks. Slovenia has a handful of LinkedIn voices and a lot of quiet expertise. The country lacks a visible, recognised cadre of "lighthouses" — people who can sit down with a sceptical SME owner for an hour and produce a *moment of joy*.
6. **The wrong story.** Public discourse oscillates between "AI will take your job" and "AI is overhyped." Both stories deflate agency. The accurate story is harder and more energising: *AI is a one-in-a-generation cognitive expansion. You will work with it. The window to learn it well is short. Tomorrow is too late.*

These six blockers map directly to what adoption data keeps showing. Microsoft's [WorkLab research](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/ai-data-drop-the-11-by-11-tipping-point) tracks a "novelty dip" between weeks three and ten of Copilot use — enthusiasm wanes before habitual use kicks in around week eleven for those who push through. Most people, without workflow-level support, do not push through; this is the [three-week cliff](/knowledge/three-week-cliff). Shopify attacked the same problem culturally by **flipping the burden of proof**: before asking for more headcount or resources, teams must show why they cannot get the work done with AI. The cultural shift, not the technology, was the lever.

## The plan: six moves Slovenia can make in months, not years

This is what a serious Slovene response looks like. Each move is concrete, fundable, and can begin before the next budget cycle.

### 1. Frontier-tool access for all, by September 2026

Treat agentic AI like the country treated Zoom and MS Teams during COVID. Procure a national framework giving **every primary, secondary and tertiary student, every teacher, every civil servant, and every employee of a registered SME** seat access to at least one frontier agentic system at the highest tier the vendor will negotiate. The working precedent is [Estonia's AI Leap](https://e-estonia.com/ai-leap-2025-estonia-sets-ai-standard-in-education/) — a public-private national programme funded jointly by the Estonian state and private partners (≈€15M/year), governed by a new AI Leap Foundation, with frontier-vendor partnerships (OpenAI, Anthropic) for tool access. Extend the same model from 20,000 students to Slovenia's entire active population.

**Fund it as a European-aligned move, not a lonely national experiment.** Slovenia can combine national NpUI follow-on funding with EU instruments that already prioritise AI skills, SME adoption, public-sector use and EuroHPC/AI Factory access — the [EU AI Continent strategy](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2025/775923/EPRS_BRI(2025)775923_EN.pdf), the Digital Decade programme, and the EuroHPC AI Factories pillar. None of these earmark "buy every citizen a frontier seat", but all three fund adjacent and complementary moves. The subscription-seat layer should be negotiated as a **national framework agreement** with €100 per active user per year as a **target ceiling**, not an assumed market price.

**Procure multi-vendor by design, and name the tension honestly.** The frontier in 2026 is American: the agentic-capability lead sits with Anthropic's **Claude** (Code and Co-work), OpenAI's **Codex app**, and Google's **Gemini** (with Antigravity) — a margin where the moment of joy will not reliably fire on second-tier tools. Slovenia cannot wait for an EU frontier lab to match that capability; the 24-month window closes long before that gap does. The strategic posture is therefore: *use the frontier we have, fund local talent on EU compute, and hedge with EU vendors where the workload demands sovereignty.* The procurement framework should run **multi-vendor by default** — those three for everyday drafting, summarising, analysis, learning and prototyping; the leading EU model (currently Mistral) as a parallel vendor, **mandatory** for sovereignty-sensitive workloads: defence-adjacent, minor-data, healthcare records, citizen-dignity decisions, classified material. Stated as a choice, this position is honest and defensible. Hidden as an assumption, it is a political target.

**Back-of-envelope budget.** Year-one cohorts: roughly 25,000 teachers, ~70,000 secondary and tertiary students, ~35,000 civil servants, plus 1–5 seats per registered SME (≈50,000–150,000 SME seats). Total: ~180,000–280,000 active seats. At a €100 target ceiling per active seat per year, the annual cost lands in the **€18–28 million** range — comparable to a single line item in Slovenia's Digital Decade allocation, smaller than the existing €110 million NpUI envelope, and an order of magnitude smaller than the productivity loss to a two-year delay. These are negotiation targets, not procurement quotes; treat them as the bar to clear, not the price to pay.

### 2. Flip the burden of proof in the public sector

A simple administrative instruction from the Prime Minister's office, modelled on the [Shopify memo](/videos/dzp0OQbElpU): **before requesting a new full-time position or external consulting engagement, every public body must document why an agentic system cannot do the work.** Not a ban on hiring. A default that forces the question. Pair with a small "agentic exemptions" team in the Ministry of Public Administration that can rule on edge cases inside two weeks. The Shopify rule changed company culture inside one quarter. There is no structural reason it could not change ministerial culture inside two.

### 3. A Slovenian "Lighthouse Corps"

Recruit 200–300 evangelists across the country — teachers, doctors, engineers, lawyers, accountants, mayors, public-sector middle managers, museum curators — and give them three things: top-tier frontier access, a small honorarium, and an explicit mandate to *light sparks*. Their job is not to train. Their job is to sit down with sceptics one afternoon a week and produce a moment of joy. Track one metric only: "first joy moments" per quarter. The Lighthouse Corps is the human network that turns access into experience and experience into contagion. It is the missing component every Slovene transformation plan has ever lacked.

### 4. Solve confidentiality and security at the platform layer, not the user layer

The single fastest way to keep institutions from blocking adoption is to remove the security objection from individual users. Sign EU-region data processing agreements with frontier vendors at the national level. Stand up a small national "agentic safety" team — three people, not thirty — to publish a simple, living playbook with explicit tiers:

- **Public / non-sensitive work** (drafts, summaries, research, learning) → frontier cloud tools, default permissive.
- **Internal business data** (operational documents, non-personal commercial data) → approved EU-region enterprise plans, with data-processing agreements and audit logging.
- **Personal data, health records, minors, defence, classified, dignity-affecting decisions** → local or EU-sovereign route (Mistral or equivalent for the cloud-acceptable subset), or no external model at all.
- **High-risk decisions** (rights, employment, benefits, healthcare outcomes) → AI assists, human decides, decision logged.

Make the default *permissive with a clear rule for exceptions*, not *restrictive with vague guidance*. Aligning the tiers with the EU AI Act's risk categories makes the playbook regulator-ready from day one. The McKinsey-style breach pattern ([Daily Digest, May 11](/digests/2026-05-11)) makes this urgent — but it is a platform problem to solve once, not a barrier each teacher should re-litigate.

### 5. Bring the SME 99% along — through chambers, not consultants

Slovenia's ~54-point gap between large and small firms is the single biggest leverage point in the economy. The chambers are the right delivery rails — GZS and OZS as the primary domestic networks, supplemented by Eurochambres-affiliated programmes and AmCham Slovenia for cross-border practice and member-firm pilots. Three concrete instruments: (a) free frontier seat access for the first year for any registered SME that completes a 2-hour "first beam" workshop; (b) a *vavčer* programme structured around **outcomes** (one shipped agentic workflow), not hours of consulting; (c) sector-specific "agentic playbooks" co-authored by Lighthouse Corps practitioners — a tourism playbook, a metals-and-machinery playbook, a logistics playbook — published openly, in Slovene.

The Klarna lesson is **not** "replace people blindly." Klarna's AI assistant did initially absorb the work of 700 customer-service agents — and Klarna [walked some of that back](https://www.entrepreneur.com/business-news/klarna-ceo-reverses-course-by-hiring-more-humans-not-ai/491396) in 2025 after customer-satisfaction dropped on complex cases. The honest lesson: a narrow, high-volume workflow can move dramatically when AI is **scoped, measured and supervised** — and quality boundaries still matter. The mid-sized Slovene manufacturer with a five-person ops team can capture proportional gains in invoicing, scheduling, supplier email, technical-doc translation — by picking the right scope, not by replacing the team.

### 6. Re-anchor the public story: this is a cognitive expansion, not a job-loss event

The Finnish education reform succeeded partly because it was sold as *liberating children's agency*, not as catching up to PISA scores. Slovenia needs the same reframing for AI. The right line — for ministers, school directors, parents, union leaders, journalists — is something like: **"This is a once-in-a-generation chance to expand what every Slovene can know and do. We can either work with it, starting now, or watch other small countries pull away. We are choosing now."** The doom story disempowers; the cognitive-expansion story conscripts. Choose the one that creates agents, not victims.

## Five questions that come up in every room

These are the questions any executive, school director or policy advisor will ask within ten minutes of being shown this plan. Here are the short, honest answers.

**What do graduates bring that AI cannot?** Taste, judgement, and the willingness to push back. Code, drafts, analyses — those are now infrastructure. We are not hiring for execution; we are hiring for [rejection competency](/knowledge/rejection-competency) and [specification quality](/knowledge/specification-quality). If a graduate cannot tell you *why* an AI output is wrong in their domain, the graduate is not yet useful. If they can, they are worth three of the old kind.

**Who actually adapts when we deploy AI?** Almost never the most technical or the most senior. The people with high agency and low ego — the ones willing to sit with the tool, get bad results, push back, and try again. Often mid-career. Often women returning to work after a break. Often not the ones you expected. If you are picking a pilot team, pick for temperament, not titles.

**Where can we trust AI without reservation, and where does a human still decide?** Trust it anywhere the [three pillars](/essays/2026-04-16-01-dark-code-and-the-three-pillars) are strong — specification, tests, code — and where the blast radius is contained. Drafting, summarising, first-pass research, schema migration with backups, refactoring against a green test suite. Reserve the human decision for hiring people, firing people, allocating capital above a threshold, anything irreversible, anything that touches the dignity of a citizen.

**Which new professions actually emerge?** Specification architects. Verification strategists. Lighthouse-style domain translators. Agent orchestrators. [Solo operators](/knowledge/solo-operator) running scope that used to require a team. We do not need to predict the catalogue perfectly — we need to make sure the *training environments* (real frontier tools, real problems) exist in Slovenia so those roles form here rather than being imported from Berlin or Tallinn.

**What awaits the entrepreneur who delays for another two years?** A strategic warning, not a measured forecast: the gap with better-prepared peers will not stay at the polite 20–30% productivity differential currently quoted in surveys. The [compounding gap](/knowledge/compounding-gap) will widen — plausibly toward **5–10×** in the workflows AI most reshapes — because early adopters are not just faster; they are recalibrating their entire mental model of what one person can ship in a day. The simplest possible advice for anyone in the room: tonight, open Claude Code or the Codex app, take one real task from tomorrow's calendar, and do it with the tool. Not a toy task. A real one. **Joy is on the other side, and so is the next decade.**

## The wager

The honest version of Slovenia's AI position is this. We have a small, educated, multilingual population. We have a credible existing strategy (NpUI 2020–2025, €110 million) that took us partway. We have one of the EU's widest internal AI adoption gaps and one of its smaller windows to close it. We do not have time for another five-year cycle. We do have time — barely — for a **24-month sprint** that puts frontier tools in everyone's hands, flips the public-sector burden of proof, builds a Lighthouse network, and re-anchors the story around cognitive expansion.

The bicycle does not wait for us to feel ready. Estonia did not wait. Finland did not wait. The question is whether Slovenia rides the next 24 months, or gets reshaped by them.

The best time to start was last September. The second best time is tomorrow morning.
